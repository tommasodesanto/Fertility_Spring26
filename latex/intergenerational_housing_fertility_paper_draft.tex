\title{Intergenerational Housing Misallocation and Fertility\\[-0.15em]
\large Compact Theory and Quantitative Lifecycle Model}
\author{Tommaso De Santo}
\date{July 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Children require goods and housing space. The space requirement matters because
family-sized housing is harder to access than small rental housing: large units
are concentrated in ownership, ownership requires down payments, and tax rules
can induce older owners to retain houses whose marginal value to them is below
the value to constrained young families. This draft has two parts. Part I gives
a compact two-period model that isolates the mechanism and delivers
goods-equivalent wedges for young access constraints, old-owner lock-in, and
fertility. Part II embeds the same mechanism in a finite-life quantitative
lifecycle model with income risk, tenure and house-size choice, down-payment
constraints, transaction costs, bequests, child aging, and a single aggregate
housing-services market.
\end{abstract}

The model combines a lifecycle fertility problem
\citep{sommerFertilityChoiceLife2016,doepkeBargainingBabiesTheory2019} with
housing tenure choice, collateral constraints, and owner-occupied housing
\citep{hendersonModelHousingTenure1983,sommerEquilibriumEffectFundamentals2013,sommerImplicationsUSTax2018}.
The compact misallocation block follows the one-market floorspace logic in
\citet{coven2024}: all housing services clear in one market, while tenure and
tax rules determine who can occupy family-sized units.

\section*{Related Literature}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Related Literature}

This paper starts from the Becker quantity-quality tradition: children are
chosen by households, and the relevant price of a child includes the resources
that child requires \citep{beckerEconomicAnalysisFertility1960,beckerInteractionQuantityQuality1973}.
The distinct object here is housing space. If a child requires housing services,
then the price of children is not summarized by wages, child care, or transfers
alone. It also depends on the household's shadow value of family-sized space.
That value can exceed the market rent when families are constrained by rental
size limits, down-payment requirements, or thin access to owner-occupied units.

The evidence that speaks most directly to completed fertility is smaller than
the evidence on current birth rates, but it is not empty. \citet{simonTamuraHigherRents2009}
use U.S. Census data from 1940--2000 and find that higher rents per room are
associated with lower fertility; their analysis of completed fertility among
older households reinforces the younger-household patterns. \citet{clarkWomenDelayFamily2012}
is an important caution: expensive U.S. housing markets delay first births by
several years, but the paper does not find a clear completed-fertility decline.
More recent work ties the timing margin back to fertility quantum. \citet{japaridzeSayourHousingAffordability2024}
argue that unaffordable homeownership reduces completed fertility by delaying
entry into motherhood and show that later first births are associated with
lower completed fertility. Two recent causal designs point in the same
direction. \citet{dettlingKearneyModernMortgage2025} study the FHA and VA
mortgage programs and find evidence that low-down-payment, long-term mortgage
access increased births during the U.S. baby boom; their Census measures of
children ever born are consistent with higher completed fertility for the
groups that were able to use the programs. \citet{vandoornikFazioRamadoraiSkrastinsHousingFertility2025}
exploit randomized Brazilian housing-credit lotteries and find that early
access to housing raises fertility, with larger lifetime effects when access
arrives earlier in the childbearing years.

The larger economics literature is mostly about current-period births, and it
should be read as evidence on mechanisms rather than as direct evidence on
final family size. \citet{lovenheimFamilyWealthShocks2013} and
\citet{dettlingHousePricesBirth2014} show that housing wealth and house-price
shocks move fertility differently for owners and nonowners: owner wealth
effects tend to raise births, while higher housing costs reduce births among
households that do not own. \citet{hacamoBabiesMortgageDeregulation2021}
finds that mortgage-market deregulation increased young households' home
purchases and childbearing, with evidence pointing to access to space rather
than income growth as the mechanism. \citet{cummingMonetaryPolicyBirth2024}
show that mortgage-rate pass-through affects births through household cash
flows. These papers establish that housing finance affects fertility behavior,
but by themselves they do not settle whether a birth-rate response is a timing
shift or a change in completed fertility.

Demographic work provides the life-cycle complement. The repeated finding is
that housing tenure, dwelling size, and residential transitions are jointly
timed with family formation. \citet{mulderBillariHomeownershipRegimes2010}
argue that homeownership regimes with difficult market entry are associated
with low fertility. In Swedish and Finnish microdata, housing conditions and
housing transitions are closely linked to first births and higher-parity births
\citep{stromHousingFirstBirths2010,kuluSteeleChildbearingHousingTransitions2013,chudnovskayaHousingContext2019}.
\citet{tocchioniChangingAssociation2021} show that the homeownership-parenthood
link in Britain has weakened as housing access has become more difficult for
young adults. This literature supports the paper's primitive that family
formation is tied to access to suitable housing, while also warning that tenure
status is endogenous to fertility plans.

Relative to this evidence, the paper's contribution is to put completed
fertility, tenure choice, down-payment constraints, and intergenerational
housing allocation in one equilibrium environment. The housing block builds on
tenure-choice and housing-macro models with collateral constraints and
owner-occupied housing \citep{hendersonModelHousingTenure1983,sommerEquilibriumEffectFundamentals2013,sommerImplicationsUSTax2018}.
The closest recent structural paper is \citet{couillardBuildBabyBuild2025},
which studies the joint choice of location, house size, and fertility and shows
that the supply of large units matters for aggregate fertility. This paper
instead focuses on who occupies family-sized space over the lifecycle: young
families can value marginal space above its market user cost because they are
constrained, while old incumbents can retain space because tax rules subsidize
staying put. That intergenerational allocation margin is the object measured by
the wedges below.

\part{Compact Analytical Model}
% ==========================================================

\section{Environment}

\textbf{Agents.} The economy is populated by overlapping generations of young
and old households. A young household has type
\[
    i=(y_i,a_i),
\]
where $y_i>0$ is disposable income and $a_i\ge0$ is entry wealth, inherited from
the previous old generation. Conditional on entry, the young household chooses
tenure, housing, completed fertility, and saving. Old households are indexed by
$j$. An old incumbent is last period's young owner and enters old age with
financial wealth, the house she bought, and an accrued taxable gain. She chooses
consumption, how much housing to retain, and the estate she leaves at exit. An
old renter carries no housing and no capital-gains wedge.

\textbf{Preferences.} Children raise both consumption needs and housing needs.
In the compact model fertility is continuous. A young household choosing
consumption $c_i$, housing $h_i$, and fertility $n_i$ has utility
\begin{equation}
u_i^Y(c_i,h_i,n_i)
=
\log(c_i-\chi n_i)+\alpha\log(h_i-\kappa n_i)+\vartheta\log n_i,
\qquad
\alpha,\vartheta,\chi,\kappa>0.
\label{eq:compact_young_utility}
\end{equation}
The term $\chi n_i$ is the goods requirement of children and $\kappa n_i$ is
their housing-space requirement. A marginal child therefore uses the same
housing stock as the parents.

Old households value consumption, housing, and bequests:
\begin{equation}
u^O(c_j^O,h_j^O,e_j)
=
\log c_j^O+\gamma\log h_j^O+\Bcal(e_j),
\qquad
\gamma>0,
\label{eq:compact_old_utility}
\end{equation}
where $e_j\ge0$ is the estate and $\Bcal$ is increasing and concave, possibly
identically zero. Bequest motives and true old housing utility are preferences;
they are not wedges.

\textbf{Housing.} There is a fixed stock $\bar H$ of divisible floorspace.
Households occupy floorspace by renting or owning. The two tenure modes have
different size limits:
\begin{equation}
\HH^R=\{h:0<h\le \bar h^R\},
\qquad
\HH^O=\{h:0<h\le \bar h^O\},
\qquad
0<\bar h^R<\bar h^O.
\label{eq:compact_size_sets}
\end{equation}
This is the family-housing segmentation: small units are available to rent, but
large family-capable units are mainly reached through ownership. All floorspace
trades in one market. The user cost of housing is $q$ and the asset price is
$P$, linked by
\begin{equation}
q=\rho P,
\qquad
\rho=r+\delta+\tau^p,
\label{eq:compact_user_cost}
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the interest rate, $\delta$ is depreciation, and $\tau^p$ is the
property-tax rate. For a given user cost $q$, a higher property tax lowers the
asset price $P$ and therefore lowers the down payment needed to buy a given
house.

\textbf{Financing.} A buyer can finance at most share $\phi\in(0,1)$ of the
purchase price. Buying $h$ units therefore requires the cash down payment
\begin{equation}
    (1-\phi)Ph.
\label{eq:compact_down_payment}
\end{equation}
Renters cannot borrow. Households can save in a bond at return $1+r$. The
compact model treats the down-payment constraint as an access constraint: it
limits the amount of owner housing a young household can occupy.

\textbf{Capital-gains taxation and step-up basis.} Selling a house triggers a
capital-gains tax. Dying with the house gives a stepped-up basis, so the accrued
gain is forgiven. In a stationary real model, the gain comes from nominal price
growth. If dollar prices grow at rate $\varpi$, a house bought one period ago
has a taxable paper gain even when its real value is unchanged. The real
flow-equivalent tax wedge per unit sold is
\begin{equation}
    \bar\ell=\tau^g\frac{P\varpi}{1+\varpi},
\label{eq:compact_gain_wedge}
\end{equation}
after normalizing the period conversion factor to one. The wedge is not a
preference and not a resource cost. It is a private tax liability avoided by
holding the house until death.

\section{Households}

Conditional on entry, young household $i$ chooses a tenure mode
$m\in\{R,O\}$, housing, fertility, consumption, and savings. For
$m\in\{R,O\}$,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
W_i^m(q)
=
\max_{c_i,h_i,n_i,a_i'}\quad
&\log(c_i-\chi n_i)+\alpha\log(h_i-\kappa n_i)+\vartheta\log n_i\\
&+\beta\left[
\1\{m=R\}V^R(a_{j'})
+\1\{m=O\}V^O(a_{j'},h_i)
\right]
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:compact_young_problem}
\end{equation}
subject to
\begin{align}
c_i+q h_i+a_i'&=y_i+a_i,
\label{eq:compact_young_budget}\\
h_i&\in\HH^m,\qquad a_i'\ge0,
\notag\\
\1\{m=O\}(1-\phi)Ph_i&\le a_i,
\label{eq:compact_owner_constraint}\\
c_i-\chi n_i&>0,\qquad h_i-\kappa n_i>0.
\notag
\end{align}
Using $P=q/\rho$, the largest owner house feasible for type $i$ is
\begin{equation}
H_i^O(q)=
\min\left\{
\bar h^O,\frac{a_i\rho}{(1-\phi)q}
\right\},
\qquad
H_i^R=\bar h^R.
\label{eq:compact_family_cap}
\end{equation}
$H_i^m$ is the effective family-housing cap. A renter is capped by the rental
stock. A buyer is capped by the owner size limit or by collateral, whichever is
shorter.

An old incumbent enters with financial wealth $a_j$, carried housing $H_j^0$,
and an accrued gain. She chooses consumption, retained housing, and an estate:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
V^O(a_j,H_j^0)
&=
\max_{c_j^O,h_j^O,e_j}
\;u^O(c_j^O,h_j^O,e_j)\\
&\text{s.t.}\quad
c_j^O+e_j+q h_j^O
=
a_j+qH_j^0-\bar\ell(H_j^0-h_j^O),
\qquad
0\le h_j^O\le H_j^0.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:compact_old_problem}
\end{equation}
Selling one unit nets $q-\bar\ell$, so stepped-up basis acts as a subsidy to
retaining the house. An old renter has no carried housing and no tax:
\begin{equation}
V^R(a_j)
=
\max_{c_j^O,h_j^O,e_j}
u^O(c_j^O,h_j^O,e_j)
\quad
\text{s.t.}\quad
c_j^O+e_j+q h_j^O=a_j,\qquad 0<h_j^O\le \bar h^R.
\label{eq:compact_old_renter_problem}
\end{equation}

\section{Entry and Equilibrium}

A potential young household can enter the housing market or take an outside
option with value $\bar W^E$. Entry has Type-I extreme-value shocks with scale
$\kappa_E$. The inside value is
\[
W_i^Y(q)=\max\{W_i^R(q),W_i^O(q)\},
\]
and the entry probability is
\begin{equation}
\pi_i^E(q)
=
\frac{\exp\{W_i^Y(q)/\kappa_E\}}
{\exp\{W_i^Y(q)/\kappa_E\}+\exp\{\bar W^E/\kappa_E\}}.
\label{eq:compact_entry}
\end{equation}

Let $F_O$ and $F_R$ be the stationary measures of old incumbents and old
renters, and let $\bar M$ be the mass of potential young households. Aggregate
floorspace demand is
\begin{equation}
H^D(q)
=
\bar M\int \pi_i^E(q)h_i(q)\,\dd G_Y(i)
+\int h_j^O(q)\,\dd F_O(j)
+\int h_j^O(q)\,\dd F_R(j).
\label{eq:compact_hd}
\end{equation}
The compact equilibrium clears the single stock:
\begin{equation}
    H^D(q)=\bar H,
    \qquad
    P=q/\rho.
\label{eq:compact_clearing}
\end{equation}
Stationarity requires current young owners to become next period's old
incumbents, current young renters to become old renters, and births plus the
renewal inflow to reproduce the next young cohort. The transition rules assign
old estates to entry wealth and are otherwise left general; the local results
below condition on the stationary cross-section.

\section{Equilibrium Wedges}

The model's sufficient statistics are marginal values of space in consumption
goods. Let $\lambda_i^m$ be the multiplier on the young household's budget,
$\theta_i^m$ the multiplier on the size limit, and $\mu_i$ the multiplier on
the owner's down-payment constraint. For renters,
\begin{equation}
\frac{\alpha(c_i^R-\chi n_i^R)}{h_i^R-\kappa n_i^R}
=
q+\zeta_i^R,
\qquad
\zeta_i^R=\frac{\theta_i^R}{\lambda_i^R}\ge0.
\label{eq:compact_renter_mv}
\end{equation}
A capped renter values one more unit of housing above the rent it would command.

For young owners, anticipation of the future capital-gains liability raises the
effective price of owning family-sized space. Under interior saving and
interior old retention,
\begin{equation}
q^O=q+\frac{\bar\ell}{1+r}.
\label{eq:compact_owner_effective_price}
\end{equation}
The owner access gap is
\begin{equation}
\zeta_i^O
=
\underbrace{\frac{\mu_i}{\lambda_i^O}(1-\phi)P}_{\zeta_i^{O,F}\text{, down-payment component}}
+
\underbrace{\frac{\theta_i^O}{\lambda_i^O}}_{\text{owner-size component}}
\ge0,
\label{eq:compact_owner_zeta}
\end{equation}
so the owner's marginal value of space is
\begin{equation}
\frac{\alpha(c_i^O-\chi n_i^O)}{h_i^O-\kappa n_i^O}
=
q^O+\zeta_i^O.
\label{eq:compact_owner_mv}
\end{equation}
When the owner size limit is slack, $\zeta_i^O=\zeta_i^{O,F}$ and the gap is a
financing gap.

The old incumbent's interior retention condition is
\begin{equation}
\frac{\gamma c_j^O}{h_j^O}=q-\bar\ell.
\label{eq:compact_old_mv}
\end{equation}
She keeps marginal space she values below the market user cost because selling
it realizes the taxable gain. An old renter has no such wedge and, at an
interior choice, values space at $q$.

The fertility condition prices the marginal child in goods. In tenure mode
$m$, with $q^R=q$ and $q^O$ given by
\eqref{eq:compact_owner_effective_price},
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{\frac{\vartheta(c_i^m-\chi n_i^m)}{n_i^m}}_{\text{willingness to pay for a marginal child}}
=
\underbrace{\chi+\kappa(q^m+\zeta_i^m)}_{\text{full price of a child}}.
\label{eq:compact_child_price}
\end{equation}
A child's space requirement is priced at the household's own shadow value of
space. A binding access constraint therefore acts as an implicit child tax
$\kappa\zeta_i^m$.

\section{Efficiency and Fertility}

The planner comparison holds the stationary cross-section, entry probabilities,
tenure assignments, savings, and real size-limit constraints fixed at the
instant of the variation. The planner counts true household preferences and
true resource costs. It does not count the avoided capital-gains tax as a social
benefit.

\begin{proposition}[Local young-old housing misallocation]
Consider a stationary equilibrium. Hold entry, tenure assignments, savings, and
real size-limit constraints fixed. Take a young owner whose owner size limit is
slack and whose financing constraint binds, so $\zeta_i^{O,F}>0$, and an old
incumbent at an interior retention choice. If one unit of existing housing can
be reassigned from the old incumbent to the young owner at zero real
implementation cost, the planner's goods-equivalent surplus is
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{(q^O+\zeta_i^{O,F})}_{\text{young buyer's marginal value}}
-
\underbrace{(q-\bar\ell)}_{\text{old incumbent's marginal value}}
=
\zeta_i^{O,F}+\frac{\bar\ell}{1+r}+\bar\ell.
\label{eq:compact_efficiency_gap}
\end{equation}
If this expression is positive, the competitive allocation does not solve the
planner's conditional allocation problem.
\end{proposition}

The market price cancels. What remains are the young household's financing gap,
the buyer's anticipated gains tax, and the old incumbent's realized gains tax.
The two tax terms are transfers, not resource costs. If implementing the
reassignment requires real cost $r_i^F$ per unit, that cost subtracts from the
surplus. If the down-payment constraint is a primitive technological
enforcement constraint and no instrument can relax it, the variation is not
feasible.

Old occupancy is not inefficient by itself. An old owner who keeps a large
house because she values it, or because she values leaving bequests, is using
housing for reasons the planner also counts. The inefficiency is the part of
retention supported by the forgiveness of capital-gains taxes at death, paired
with young households who value family-sized space above its market user cost.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.62\textwidth]{example_misallocation_only.pdf}
\caption{Local misallocation. A collateral-capped young buyer values marginal
space at $q^O+\zeta^{O,F}$, while a locked-in old incumbent values retained
space at $q-\bar\ell$. The vertical distance is the goods-equivalent surplus
from a compensated marginal reassignment.}
\label{fig:compact_misallocation}
\end{figure}

The same cap determines a fertility response. Fix a tenure mode and a household
whose housing is pinned at the effective family-housing cap $H$. Concentrating
the savings rule into the budget, fertility along the binding cap solves
\[
\frac{\vartheta}{n}
=
\frac{(1+k)\chi}{w-q^mH-\chi n}
+
\frac{\alpha\kappa}{H-\kappa n},
\]
where $w=y+a$ and $k$ is the savings load on adult consumption. Differentiating
with respect to the cap gives
\begin{equation}
\frac{\dd n}{\dd H}
=
\frac{
\dfrac{\alpha\kappa}{(H-\kappa n)^2}
-
\dfrac{(1+k)\chi q^m}{(w-q^mH-\chi n)^2}
}{
\dfrac{\vartheta}{n^2}
+\dfrac{(1+k)\chi^2}{(w-q^mH-\chi n)^2}
+\dfrac{\alpha\kappa^2}{(H-\kappa n)^2}
}.
\label{eq:compact_fertility_cap_derivative}
\end{equation}
Relaxing access raises fertility when the space-relief force dominates the
budget-tightening force. A useful sufficient condition along every binding cap
is
\begin{equation}
    \chi\le \frac{(1+k)\kappa q^m}{\alpha}.
\label{eq:compact_equal_scaling}
\end{equation}
The derivative connects the misallocation wedge to fertility: policies that
raise the effective family-housing cap matter only for households whose binding
constraint they actually relax.

\part{Quantitative Lifecycle Model}
% ==========================================================

\section{Purpose and Timing}

The quantitative model embeds the same mechanism in a finite-life economy. It
has the standard lifecycle structure: households age, face income risk, save,
choose whether to rent or own, choose housing size, decide fertility, and
eventually leave bequests. The housing market has one aggregate user cost and
one asset price. Tenure matters because it changes the size menu, financing
constraints, transaction costs, and tax exposure.

Time is discrete. Households enter adult life at age $a=1$, retire after age
$J_R$, and die at the end of age $J$. The individual state at the beginning of a
period is
\begin{equation}
x=(b,z,d,a,n,s).
\label{eq:quant_state}
\end{equation}
Here $b$ is liquid wealth, $z\in\Zset$ is idiosyncratic productivity, $d$ is
tenure and housing position, $a$ is age, $n$ is completed fertility, and $s$ is
the child-at-home state. Tenure belongs to
\begin{equation}
\Dset=\{R,O_1,\ldots,O_K\}.
\label{eq:quant_tenure_set}
\end{equation}
$R$ denotes renting. $O_k$ denotes ownership of a house with physical size
$H_k$, where $H_1<\cdots<H_K$. Renters choose housing continuously up to
$\bar h^R$; owners choose from the discrete ladder. The ladder is the
quantitative version of the family-housing segmentation in Part I.

Income for working households is
\begin{equation}
y(a,z)=(1-\tau_{\mathrm{pay}})w e_a z,
\qquad
z'\sim \Pi^z(\cdot|z),
\qquad
a\le J_R,
\label{eq:quant_income}
\end{equation}
and retired households receive pension income $p^{\mathrm{ret}}$. The wage is
common apart from age and productivity because the quantitative mechanism is
housing access, tenure, and lifecycle wealth.

\section{Preferences and Bequests}

Let $m(n,s)$ be the number of dependent children living at home. Completed
fertility $n$ and current dependents differ because children eventually mature.
Household needs are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bar c(n,s)&=\bar c_0+\bar c_n m(n,s),\\
\bar h(n,s)&=\bar h_0
+\bar h_{\mathrm{jump}}\1\{m(n,s)\ge1\}
+\bar h_n m(n,s).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:quant_child_needs}
\end{equation}
The first child can create a discrete housing-space jump; additional dependent
children raise the required space linearly.

Flow utility is Stone-Geary over consumption and housing services, wrapped in
CRRA:
\begin{equation}
u(c,\mathbf h;n,s)
=
\frac{
\left[
(c-\bar c(n,s))^\alpha
(\mathbf h-\bar h(n,s))^{1-\alpha}
\right]^{1-\sigma}
}{1-\sigma}
+\psi_{\mathrm{child}}m(n,s),
\label{eq:quant_flow_utility}
\end{equation}
with utility equal to $-\infty$ when either residual is nonpositive. Housing
services differ by tenure:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf h=
\begin{cases}
h^R, & d=R,\\
\chi_O H_k, & d=O_k,
\end{cases}
\qquad
\chi_O>0.
\label{eq:quant_housing_services}
\end{equation}
In market clearing, owners demand the physical quantity $H_k$, not the service
quantity $\chi_OH_k$.

At death, households receive warm-glow bequest utility. Let $W^B$ be
bequeathable wealth after liquidation costs and taxes. Bequest utility is
\begin{equation}
\Bcal(W^B,n)
=
\theta_0(1+\theta_n n)
\frac{
(\theta_1+\max\{W^B,0\})^{1-\sigma}
-\theta_1^{1-\sigma}
}{1-\sigma}.
\label{eq:quant_bequest}
\end{equation}
The parameter $\theta_n$ lets completed fertility affect the desired estate
even after children have left the household.

\section{Housing, Finance, and Taxes}

There is one aggregate housing-services market. The user cost $q$ and asset
price $P$ satisfy
\begin{equation}
q=(r+\delta+\tau^p)P.
\label{eq:quant_user_cost}
\end{equation}
Housing supply is upward sloping:
\begin{equation}
H^S(q)=H_0\left(\frac{q}{\bar q}\right)^\eta.
\label{eq:quant_supply}
\end{equation}
The fixed-stock compact model is the limiting case $\eta=0$ around the
benchmark stock.

Renters choose $c,h^R,b'$ subject to
\begin{equation}
c+q h^R+b'=R b+y(a,z),
\qquad
0\le h^R\le \bar h^R,
\qquad
b'\ge0,
\label{eq:quant_renter_budget}
\end{equation}
where $R=1+r$. Owners of rung $H_k$ choose $c,b'$ subject to the owner branch
budget and the borrowing limit described below. Owners pay maintenance and
property taxes through $(\delta+\tau^p)PH_k$.

At origination, a buyer can finance at most share $\phi$ of house value, so the
required down payment is
\begin{equation}
    (1-\phi)PH_k.
\label{eq:quant_dp}
\end{equation}
The post-purchase liquid position must satisfy
\begin{equation}
b'\ge -\phi P H_k.
\label{eq:quant_ltv}
\end{equation}
An incumbent owner who keeps the same house is not forced to delever after a
price change:
\begin{equation}
b'\ge \min\{b,-\phi P H_k\}.
\label{eq:quant_incumbent_ltv}
\end{equation}
This convention constrains origination but lets incumbency carry state
dependence.

Housing adjustment is costly. Let $\psi^s$ be a proportional sale cost. When
the capital-gains block is active, the state of an owner also includes a tax
basis $B^p$. A sale of house $H_k$ triggers tax
\begin{equation}
\Tcal^g(PH_k,B^p)
=
\tau^g\max\{PH_k-B^p-\bar g,0\},
\label{eq:quant_capital_gains_tax}
\end{equation}
where $\bar g$ is the statutory exclusion. Net sale proceeds are
\begin{equation}
\Omega(H_k,B^p)
=(1-\psi^s)PH_k-\Tcal^g(PH_k,B^p).
\label{eq:quant_sale_proceeds}
\end{equation}
At death, stepped-up basis resets the heir's basis to market value, so accrued
gains are not taxed. This is the quantitative counterpart of the old-owner
lock-in wedge $\bar\ell$ in Part I.

\section{Fertility and Child Aging}

Fertility is chosen during the fertile window
\[
a\in\{A_f^{\mathrm{start}},\ldots,A_f^{\mathrm{end}}\}.
\]
A childless household chooses
\begin{equation}
n'\in\Nset=\{0,1,\ldots,\bar n\}.
\label{eq:quant_fertility_set}
\end{equation}
The option $n'=0$ means no birth in the current period. A positive choice fixes
completed fertility forever and moves the household into the first
dependent-child state. A household that reaches the end of the fertile window
without a positive choice remains childless.

Let $s=0$ denote no dependent children and let $s=1,\ldots,S_c$ denote
dependent-child stages. After a positive fertility choice, the household enters
$s=1$. Children age stochastically through the stages according to $\Pi^c$.
While children are at home, $m(n,s)=n$; after maturity, $m(n,s)=0$. Completed
fertility still matters through bequest utility.

\section{Household Problem}

Within the period, the household first faces fertility if eligible, then
chooses tenure and housing position, then chooses consumption, rental housing,
and saving. Let the continuation value after income risk and child aging be
\begin{equation}
\bar V_{a+1}(b',d',z,n,s)
=
\sum_{z'}\sum_{s'}
\Pi^z(z'|z)\Pi^c(s'|s,n)
V_{a+1}(b',z',d',n,s').
\label{eq:quant_continuation}
\end{equation}
The terminal value is
\[
V_{J+1}(b,z,d,n,s)=\Bcal(W^B,n).
\]

Conditional on renting, the branch value is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
V_a^R(b,z,n,s)
=
\max_{c,h^R,b'}\quad
&u(c,h^R;n,s)+\beta \bar V_{a+1}(b',R,z,n,s)\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
&c+q h^R+b'=Rb+y(a,z),\\
&0\le h^R\le\bar h^R,\qquad b'\ge0.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:quant_renter_problem}
\end{equation}
Conditional on owning rung $H_k$ after any adjustment, the branch value is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
V_a^{O_k}(\tilde b,z,n,s)
=
\max_{c,b'}\quad
&u(c,\chi_OH_k;n,s)+\beta \bar V_{a+1}(b',O_k,z,n,s)\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
&c+(\delta+\tau^p)PH_k+b'=R\tilde b+y(a,z),\\
&b'\ge \underline b^O_k.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:quant_owner_problem}
\end{equation}
The lower bound $\underline b^O_k$ is the origination or incumbent constraint
in \eqref{eq:quant_ltv}--\eqref{eq:quant_incumbent_ltv}, depending on whether
the household buys or keeps the current house.

Let $H(R)=0$ and $H(O_k)=H_k$. The liquid wealth available after housing
adjustment is
\begin{equation}
\tilde b(b,d,d')
=
b
+\1\{d\in\Oset,\ d'\neq d\}\Omega(H(d),B^p)
-\1\{d'\in\Oset,\ d'\neq d\}PH(d').
\label{eq:quant_adjusted_wealth}
\end{equation}
Without the capital-gains block, $\Omega(H(d),B^p)=(1-\psi^s)PH(d)$.

The tenure branch value for feasible $d'$ is
\begin{equation}
\widetilde V_a^T(d'|b,z,d,n,s)
=
\begin{cases}
V_a^R(\tilde b(b,d,R),z,n,s), & d'=R,\\
V_a^{O_k}(\tilde b(b,d,O_k),z,n,s), & d'=O_k.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:quant_tenure_branch}
\end{equation}
Tenure and owner-rung choices have Type-I extreme-value shocks with scale
$\kappa_T$:
\begin{equation}
\pi_a^T(d'|x)
=
\frac{\exp\{\widetilde V_a^T(d'|x)/\kappa_T\}}
{\sum_{\hat d\in\Dset(x)}
\exp\{\widetilde V_a^T(\hat d|x)/\kappa_T\}},
\label{eq:quant_tenure_probs}
\end{equation}
and the inclusive tenure value is
\begin{equation}
V_a^T(x)
=
\kappa_T\log
\sum_{\hat d\in\Dset(x)}
\exp\{\widetilde V_a^T(\hat d|x)/\kappa_T\}.
\label{eq:quant_tenure_iv}
\end{equation}

For a childless household in the fertile window, the value of fertility option
$n'$ is
\begin{equation}
\widetilde V_a^F(n'|b,z,d)
=
V_a^T(b,z,d,n',s(n')),
\qquad
s(n')=\1\{n'>0\}.
\label{eq:quant_fert_branch}
\end{equation}
With Type-I extreme-value shocks of scale $\kappa_F$,
\begin{equation}
\pi_a^F(n'|b,z,d)
=
\frac{\exp\{\widetilde V_a^F(n'|b,z,d)/\kappa_F\}}
{\sum_{\hat n\in\Nset}
\exp\{\widetilde V_a^F(\hat n|b,z,d)/\kappa_F\}}.
\label{eq:quant_fert_probs}
\end{equation}
Outside the fertile window, or after a positive fertility choice, this stage is
degenerate and $V_a=V_a^T$.

\section{Stationary Equilibrium}

Let $g$ be the stationary distribution of adult households over states. Adult
mass is normalized to one for the benchmark quantitative exercise:
\begin{equation}
    \int dg(x)=1.
\label{eq:quant_mass_norm}
\end{equation}
This normalization fixes the demographic envelope and lets fertility change
household composition, tenure, and housing demand.

\begin{definition}[Stationary equilibrium]
A stationary equilibrium is a user cost and asset price $(q,P)$, value
functions and policies, tenure and fertility probabilities, a stationary
distribution $g$, and aggregate housing quantities $(H^D,H^S)$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item households solve the branch problems
\eqref{eq:quant_renter_problem}--\eqref{eq:quant_owner_problem}, tenure choice
is given by \eqref{eq:quant_tenure_probs}, fertility choice is given by
\eqref{eq:quant_fert_probs}, and terminal utility is \eqref{eq:quant_bequest};
\item the distribution $g$ is invariant under policies, income transitions,
fertility shocks, child aging, deterministic aging, death, and entry of new
adult households;
\item aggregate physical housing demand is
\begin{equation}
H^D
=
\int
\left[
\1\{d=R\}h^R(x)
+\sum_{k=1}^K\1\{d=O_k\}H_k
\right]\dd g(x);
\label{eq:quant_hd}
\end{equation}
\item the housing market clears and the user-cost relation holds:
\begin{equation}
H^D=H^S(q),
\qquad
q=(r+\delta+\tau^p)P;
\label{eq:quant_clearing}
\end{equation}
\item payroll taxes, property taxes, capital-gains taxes, pensions, and any
rebates satisfy the government accounting rule specified for the calibration or
counterfactual.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\section{Counterfactuals}

The policy experiments should map directly into the primitive access margins:
\begin{enumerate}
\item relax down-payment constraints by increasing $\phi$ or giving targeted
down-payment resources to young families;
\item expand family-sized rental access by increasing $\bar h^R$ or reducing
the premium on large rental units;
\item reduce transaction costs $\psi^s$;
\item reform capital-gains taxation and step-up basis by changing $\tau^g$,
$\bar g$, or the treatment of gains at death;
\item increase housing supply through $H_0$ or $\eta$;
\item combine supply expansion with access policies to separate price effects
from allocation effects.
\end{enumerate}
Each counterfactual should report fertility, childlessness, ownership,
young-family housing, old-owner retention, prices, and welfare. The compact
model says where the effects should be strongest: households with positive
family-housing access gaps and policies that pass through to the binding cap.

\section{What Carries Over from the Compact Model}

The quantitative model changes the state space but not the mechanism. In the
compact model, a constrained young household prices a child's housing need at
$q^m+\zeta_i^m$, while an old locked-in owner values retained marginal space at
$q-\bar\ell$. In the quantitative model these are not single closed-form
objects for every state, but they remain the diagnostic objects:
\[
\text{young family-space shadow value}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\text{old retention shadow value}.
\]
The calibration must therefore discipline not only aggregate fertility and
aggregate ownership, but also the distribution of housing space by age, tenure,
and child status. A model that matches total ownership while putting too much
family-sized housing with unconstrained households would miss the mechanism.
