\title{Housing Access and Fertility\\[-0.15em]
\large Compact Theory and Preliminary Quantitative Results}
\author{Tommaso De Santo}
\date{July 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Children require goods and housing space. Family-sized rentals are limited,
while access to owner housing requires liquid wealth and borrowing capacity.
Older households meanwhile hold much of the large-home stock. I study how this
allocation of space changes the private price of children. A compact
overlapping-generations model expresses rental limits, down-payment constraints,
and old-owner retention as goods-equivalent wedges. It separates changes in
family size among incumbent households from changes due to housing-market entry
and tenure switching. A finite-life quantitative model adds income risk,
saving, tenure and house-size choice, transaction costs, bequests, and children
aging out of the household. The current calibration suggests that access to
family-sized space is valuable to households near the fertility margin, while
the timing of purchases is central to the response of births to housing policy.
\end{abstract}

\subsection*{Status of this draft}

This document is an extended research update rather than a completed paper.
The compact model has been checked independently, and the results stated below
include the assumptions needed for the derivations. The quantitative sections
report a July 9, 2026 calibration snapshot. Two implementation corrections are
underway: current housing choices must enter several moments contemporaneously,
and the first age cell must use the intended earnings profile. Both corrections
affect targeted moments, so the calibration and all counterfactual magnitudes
remain provisional until the model is re-estimated.

The quantitative environment below follows the specification used to produce
the snapshot: the owner premium applies to usable housing space, bequest utility
is not normalized across family-size states, and all owners face the same debt
floor. The policy exercises are preliminary patterns from an internally
consistent diagnostic runtime. They will be rerun from the corrected and
re-estimated baseline.

\section{Introduction}

A child changes a household's housing problem. It is not only another set of
goods to buy, or more time to spend at home. It is often a need for a different
dwelling: another bedroom, a larger living space, or a unit where raising a
child is feasible. In many high-cost cities this margin is hard to adjust.
Large rental units are scarce, family-sized homes are concentrated in
ownership, and ownership requires down payments and borrowing capacity at ages
when many households have little wealth.

This paper studies whether those space and financial constraints affect final
family size and the number of births generated by a cohort. That distinction
matters. A housing shock can move births across years without changing
completed fertility. It can also change local births by changing who enters a
high-cost housing market, even if completed fertility among incumbent
households moves little. The question here is when housing constraints change
completed fertility, when they mainly change entry and local scale, and how
both effects depend on who can access family-sized space over the lifecycle.

The mechanism has three parts. First, children raise the household's demand for
space. If the rental market does not offer sufficiently large units, the shadow
price of an additional room can exceed the posted rent. Second, the natural
substitute is ownership, but buying a family-sized home requires liquidity and
credit. A young household may value the larger unit and still be unable to
purchase it. Third, older owners may remain in large homes even when their own
marginal value of space is low, because transaction costs and tax rules
subsidize staying put. Housing scarcity then shows up not only as a high price
of space, but also as a mismatch between who occupies family-sized units and
who values them for fertility.

A compact two-period model expresses the rental space constraint, the ownership
finance constraint, old-owner lock-in, and the fertility distortion as
goods-equivalent wedges. I then embed the same economics in a finite-life
quantitative model. The model combines
lifecycle fertility choice
\citep{sommerFertilityChoiceLife2016,doepkeBargainingBabiesTheory2019} with
housing tenure choice, collateral constraints, and owner-occupied housing
\citep{hendersonModelHousingTenure1983,sommerEquilibriumEffectFundamentals2013,sommerImplicationsUSTax2018}.
The compact housing block follows the one-market floorspace logic in
\citet{coven2024}: all housing services clear in one market, while tenure,
borrowing, and tax rules determine who can actually occupy family-sized units.

The current quantitative exercise is informative about the mechanism but not
yet a final estimate of its size. The July snapshot places substantial
fertility-response weight on households constrained by the rental-size limit,
and it produces a sharp coincidence of births and purchases near the end of the
fertile window. It also misses early ownership, young liquid wealth, and
old-age exit from ownership. The first two misses are affected by the
implementation corrections described above. I therefore use the current
calibration to organize the evidence and the next estimation step, not to make
a settled quantitative claim.

\section{Related Literature}

The paper connects work on fertility choice to work on housing tenure and the
allocation of large homes. Since \citet{beckerEconomicAnalysisFertility1960}
and \citet{beckerInteractionQuantityQuality1973}, economics has treated
children as choice variables that enter preferences and require resources.
Quantitative versions place that choice in lifecycle environments with income
risk, saving, timing, and intrahousehold bargaining
\citep{jonesSchoonbroodtTertilt2010,sommerFertilityChoiceLife2016,doepkeBargainingBabiesTheory2019}.
Recent work emphasizes that the U.S. fertility decline is difficult to explain
with one postponement margin or period shock
\citep{kearneyPuzzleFallingUS2022}.

Housing prices, housing wealth, and mortgage finance also move fertility
behavior. \citet{dettlingHousePricesBirth2014} find opposite house-price
effects for owners and nonowners, \citet{lovenheimFamilyWealthShocks2013} find
that housing wealth raises homeowners' fertility, and
\citet{cummingMonetaryPolicyBirth2024} show that mortgage-rate pass-through
affects births through cash flow. \citet{hacamoBabiesMortgageDeregulation2021}
and \citet{dettlingKearneyModernMortgage2025} connect mortgage access to home
purchases and childbearing. In equilibrium,
\citet{couillardBuildBabyBuild2025} show that the supply of large units matters
for aggregate fertility.

The outcome here is closer to completed fertility than to a current birth
rate. \citet{simonTamuraHigherRents2009} find that rents per room predict lower
fertility, including in completed-fertility specifications.
\citet{clarkWomenDelayFamily2012} provides an important caution: expensive
housing markets delay first births but do not clearly reduce completed
fertility. More recent evidence links housing access to both timing and final
family size
\citep{japaridzeSayourHousingAffordability2024,dettlingKearneyModernMortgage2025,vandoornikFazioRamadoraiSkrastinsHousingFertility2025}.
I use this evidence to keep three objects separate: current births, completed
fertility among incumbent households, and cohort births after housing-market
entry responds.\footnote{Life-course evidence documents that fertility,
homeownership, dwelling size, and residential transitions move together
\citep{mulderBillariHomeownershipRegimes2010,kuluSteeleChildbearingHousingTransitions2013,chudnovskayaHousingContext2019}.
The model treats those joint transitions as economic choices over the
lifecycle.}

The closest housing-allocation paper is \citet{coven2024}. Their model studies
how property taxes, capitalization, and financial constraints allocate housing
between older homeowners and younger constrained households. I add children to
that allocation problem. Family-sized space held by an incumbent can then
change the private price of children for a young household. The retention force
also relates to mortgage lock-in and housing ladders
\citep{fonsecaMortgageLockMobilityLabor2023,fonsecaLiuMabilleUnlockingMortgage2026}.
My baseline quantitative model uses transaction costs rather than fixed-rate
mortgage lock-in, but the allocation logic is similar.

The quantitative housing block builds on models in which tenure, collateral
constraints, and owner-occupied housing shape household behavior
\citep{hendersonModelHousingTenure1983,sommerEquilibriumEffectFundamentals2013,sommerImplicationsUSTax2018}.
It also relates to spatial work in which housing supply and local prices
allocate households across markets
\citep{rosenWageBasedIndexes1979,robackWagesRentsQuality1982,gyourkoSuperstarCities2013,hsiehHousingConstraintsSpatial2019}.
The present model keeps one housing market so that tenure, family size, and the
allocation of space remain the central margins.

\section{Environment}

\textbf{Agents.} The economy is populated by overlapping generations of young
and old households. A young household has type
\[
    i=(y_i,a_i),
\]
where $y_i>0$ is disposable income and $a_i\ge0$ is entry wealth, inherited from
the previous old generation. Conditional on entry, the young household chooses
tenure, housing, completed fertility, and saving. Old households are indexed by
$j$. An old incumbent is last period's young owner and enters old age with
financial wealth, the house she bought, and an accrued taxable gain. She chooses
consumption, how much housing to retain, and the estate she leaves at exit. An
old renter carries no housing and no capital-gains wedge.

\textbf{Masses and demography.} At the start of period $t$, a mass $M_t$ of
potential young households is drawn from a distribution $G_{Y,t}$. A potential
young household either enters the modeled housing market or takes an outside
option. Nonentrants live outside the modeled market in both periods. Current
young entrants become next period's old households, and the next potential-young
cohort is formed by births plus a renewal inflow $R_t$. The stationary cohort
mass is denoted by $\bar M$.

\textbf{Preferences.} Children raise both consumption needs and housing needs.
In the compact model fertility is continuous. A young household choosing
consumption $c_i$, housing $h_i$, and fertility $n_i$ has utility
\begin{equation}
u_i^Y(c_i,h_i,n_i)
=
\log(c_i-\chi n_i)+\alpha\log(h_i-\kappa n_i)+\vartheta\log n_i,
\qquad
\alpha,\vartheta,\chi,\kappa>0.
\label{eq:compact_young_utility}
\end{equation}
The term $\chi n_i$ is the goods requirement of children and $\kappa n_i$ is
their housing-space requirement. A marginal child therefore uses the same
housing stock as the parents.

Old households value consumption, housing, and bequests:
\begin{equation}
u^O(c_j^O,h_j^O,e_j)
=
\log c_j^O+\gamma\log h_j^O+\Bcal(e_j),
\qquad
\gamma>0,
\label{eq:compact_old_utility}
\end{equation}
where $e_j\ge0$ is the estate and $\Bcal$ is increasing and concave, possibly
identically zero. Bequest motives and housing utility in old age are
preferences, not wedges. The closed-form fertility comparison below either
uses $\Bcal(e)=b\log e$ with an interior bequest or sets $\Bcal\equiv0$; the
allocation wedge itself does not require that specialization.

\textbf{Housing.} There is a fixed stock $\bar H$ of divisible floorspace.
Households occupy floorspace by renting or owning. The two tenure modes have
different size limits:
\begin{equation}
\HH^R=\{h:0<h\le \bar h^R\},
\qquad
\HH^O=\{h:0<h\le \bar h^O\},
\qquad
0<\bar h^R<\bar h^O.
\label{eq:compact_size_sets}
\end{equation}
This is the family-housing segmentation: small units are available to rent, but
large family-capable units are mainly reached through ownership. All floorspace
trades in one market. The user cost of housing is $q$ and the asset price is
$P$, linked by
\begin{equation}
q=\rho P,
\qquad
\rho=r+\delta+\tau^p,
\label{eq:compact_user_cost}
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the interest rate, $\delta$ is depreciation, and $\tau^p$ is the
property-tax rate. For a given user cost $q$, a higher property tax lowers the
asset price $P$ and therefore lowers the down payment needed to buy a given
house.

\textbf{Financing.} A buyer can finance at most a share $\phi\in(0,1)$ of the
purchase price. Buying $h$ units therefore requires the cash down payment
\begin{equation}
    (1-\phi)Ph.
\label{eq:compact_down_payment}
\end{equation}
Renters cannot borrow. Households can save in a bond at return $1+r$. The
compact model treats the down-payment constraint as an access constraint: it
limits the amount of owner housing a young household can occupy.

\textbf{Capital-gains taxation and step-up basis.} Selling a house triggers a
capital-gains tax. Dying with the house gives a stepped-up basis, so the accrued
gain is forgiven. In a stationary real model, the gain comes from nominal price
growth. If dollar prices grow at rate $\varpi$, a house bought one period ago
has a taxable paper gain even when its real value is unchanged. The real
flow-equivalent tax wedge per unit sold is
\begin{equation}
    \bar\ell=\tau^g\frac{P\varpi}{1+\varpi},
\label{eq:compact_gain_wedge}
\end{equation}
after normalizing the period conversion factor to one. The wedge is a private
tax liability avoided by holding the house until death. It is neither a
preference nor a resource cost.

\section{Households}

Conditional on entry, young household $i$ chooses a tenure mode
$m\in\{R,O\}$, housing, fertility, consumption, and savings. For
$m\in\{R,O\}$,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
W_i^m(q)
=
\max_{c_i,h_i,n_i,a_i'}\quad
&\log(c_i-\chi n_i)+\alpha\log(h_i-\kappa n_i)+\vartheta\log n_i\\
&+\beta\left[
\1\{m=R\}V^R(a_{j'})
+\1\{m=O\}V^O(a_{j'},h_i)
\right]
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:compact_young_problem}
\end{equation}
subject to
\begin{align}
c_i+q h_i+a_i'&=y_i+a_i,
\label{eq:compact_young_budget}\\
h_i&\in\HH^m,\qquad a_i'\ge0,
\notag\\
\1\{m=O\}(1-\phi)Ph_i&\le a_i,
\label{eq:compact_owner_constraint}\\
c_i-\chi n_i&>0,\qquad h_i-\kappa n_i>0.
\notag
\end{align}
The young household's choices generate the old state. A renter carries bond
wealth only. An owner carries both the house and the mortgage position:
\begin{equation}
a_{j'}=(1+r)a_i'-\1\{m=O\}q h_i,
\qquad
H_{j'}^0=\1\{m=O\}h_i.
\label{eq:compact_young_old_transition}
\end{equation}
This transition makes the future tax liability part of the current owner
choice rather than an exogenous tenure shifter.

Using $P=q/\rho$, the largest owner house feasible for type $i$ is
\begin{equation}
H_i^O(q)=
\min\left\{
\bar h^O,\frac{a_i\rho}{(1-\phi)q}
\right\},
\qquad
H_i^R=\bar h^R.
\label{eq:compact_family_cap}
\end{equation}
$H_i^m$ is the effective family-housing cap. A renter is capped by the rental
stock. A buyer is capped by the owner size limit or by collateral, whichever is
tighter.

An old incumbent enters with financial wealth $a_j$, carried housing $H_j^0$,
and an accrued gain. She chooses consumption, retained housing, and an estate:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
V^O(a_j,H_j^0)
&=
\max_{c_j^O,h_j^O,e_j}
\;u^O(c_j^O,h_j^O,e_j)\\
&\text{s.t.}\quad
c_j^O+e_j+q h_j^O
=
a_j+qH_j^0-\bar\ell(H_j^0-h_j^O),
\qquad
0\le h_j^O\le H_j^0.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:compact_old_problem}
\end{equation}
Selling one unit nets $q-\bar\ell$, so stepped-up basis acts as a subsidy to
retaining the house. An old renter has no carried housing and no tax:
\begin{equation}
V^R(a_j)
=
\max_{c_j^O,h_j^O,e_j}
u^O(c_j^O,h_j^O,e_j)
\quad
\text{s.t.}\quad
c_j^O+e_j+q h_j^O=a_j,\qquad 0<h_j^O\le \bar h^R.
\label{eq:compact_old_renter_problem}
\end{equation}

\begin{assumption}[Regularity at the saving and retention margins]
\label{ass:compact_regularity}
The gains wedge satisfies $0\le\bar\ell<q$. A generated old owner has positive
liquidation wealth, saves in youth, and chooses retention in the interior:
$a_i'>0$, $a_{j'}+(q-\bar\ell)H_{j'}^0>0$, and
$0<h_{j'}^O<H_{j'}^0$. Whenever an old-renter first-order condition is used,
that housing choice is also interior.
\end{assumption}

The assumption keeps the marginal owner price below smooth. At a saving or
retention corner, the wedge decomposition uses a type-specific continuation
price rather than the closed form in
eq.~\eqref{eq:compact_owner_effective_price}.

\section{Entry and Equilibrium}

A potential young household can enter the housing market or take an outside
option with value $\bar W^E$. Entry has Type-I extreme-value shocks with scale
$\kappa_E$. The inside value is
\[
W_i^Y(q)=\max\{W_i^R(q),W_i^O(q)\},
\]
and the entry probability is
\begin{equation}
\pi_i^E(q)
=
\frac{\exp\{W_i^Y(q)/\kappa_E\}}
{\exp\{W_i^Y(q)/\kappa_E\}+\exp\{\bar W^E/\kappa_E\}}.
\label{eq:compact_entry}
\end{equation}

Let $n_i^Y(q)$ be fertility in the inside tenure chosen by type $i$, and let
$\bar n_i^E$ be fertility in the outside option. Births generated by the
potential-young cohort are
\begin{equation}
N(q)
=
\bar M\int
\left[
\pi_i^E(q)n_i^Y(q)
+(1-\pi_i^E(q))\bar n_i^E
\right]\dd G_Y(i).
\label{eq:compact_cohort_births}
\end{equation}
The baseline normalization sets $\bar n_i^E=0$, but the entry-margin terms
below are written so that outside fertility can be restored by replacing inside
fertility with the gap $n_i^Y-\bar n_i^E$. In a stationary demographic
equilibrium, births and renewal close the young cohort:
\begin{equation}
    N+\bar R=\bar M.
\label{eq:compact_demographic_closure}
\end{equation}
The local formulas below hold $(\bar M,G_Y,F_O,F_R,\bar R)$ fixed. A full
demographic counterfactual instead recomputes the stationary fixed point, or
else states which object is held fixed and which object absorbs the birth
change.

Let $F_O$ and $F_R$ be the stationary measures of old incumbents and old
renters. Aggregate floorspace demand is
\begin{equation}
H^D(q)
=
\bar M\int \pi_i^E(q)h_i(q)\,\dd G_Y(i)
+\int h_j^O(q)\,\dd F_O(j)
+\int h_j^O(q)\,\dd F_R(j).
\label{eq:compact_hd}
\end{equation}
The compact equilibrium clears the single stock:
\begin{equation}
    H^D(q)=\bar H,
    \qquad
    P=q/\rho.
\label{eq:compact_clearing}
\end{equation}
Stationarity requires current young owners to become next period's old
incumbents, current young renters to become old renters, and births plus the
renewal inflow to reproduce the next young cohort. The transition rules map old
estates into entry wealth. They otherwise remain general, and the local results
condition on the stationary cross-section.

\section{Equilibrium Wedges}

The model's sufficient statistics are marginal values of space in consumption
goods. Let $\lambda_i^m$ be the multiplier on the young household's budget,
$\theta_i^m$ the multiplier on the size limit, and $\mu_i$ the multiplier on
the owner's down-payment constraint. For renters,
\begin{equation}
\frac{\alpha(c_i^R-\chi n_i^R)}{h_i^R-\kappa n_i^R}
=
q+\zeta_i^R,
\qquad
\zeta_i^R=\frac{\theta_i^R}{\lambda_i^R}\ge0.
\label{eq:compact_renter_mv}
\end{equation}
A capped renter values one more unit of housing above the rent it would command.

For young owners, anticipation of the future capital-gains liability raises the
effective price of owning family-sized space. Under
Assumption~\ref{ass:compact_regularity},
\begin{equation}
q^O=q+\frac{\bar\ell}{1+r}.
\label{eq:compact_owner_effective_price}
\end{equation}
The owner access gap is
\begin{equation}
\zeta_i^O
=
\underbrace{\frac{\mu_i}{\lambda_i^O}(1-\phi)P}_{\zeta_i^{O,F}\text{, down-payment component}}
+
\underbrace{\frac{\theta_i^O}{\lambda_i^O}}_{\text{owner-size component}}
\ge0,
\label{eq:compact_owner_zeta}
\end{equation}
so the owner's marginal value of space is
\begin{equation}
\frac{\alpha(c_i^O-\chi n_i^O)}{h_i^O-\kappa n_i^O}
=
q^O+\zeta_i^O.
\label{eq:compact_owner_mv}
\end{equation}
When the owner size limit is slack, $\zeta_i^O=\zeta_i^{O,F}$ and the gap is a
financing gap.

The old incumbent's interior retention condition is
\begin{equation}
\frac{\gamma c_j^O}{h_j^O}=q-\bar\ell.
\label{eq:compact_old_mv}
\end{equation}
She keeps marginal space she values below the market user cost because selling
it realizes the taxable gain. An old renter has no such wedge and, at an
interior choice, values space at $q$.

The fertility condition prices the marginal child in goods. In tenure mode
$m$, with $q^R=q$ and $q^O$ given by
\eqref{eq:compact_owner_effective_price},
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{\frac{\vartheta(c_i^m-\chi n_i^m)}{n_i^m}}_{\text{willingness to pay for a marginal child}}
=
\underbrace{\chi+\kappa(q^m+\zeta_i^m)}_{\text{full price of a child}}.
\label{eq:compact_child_price}
\end{equation}
A child's space requirement is priced at the household's own shadow value of
space. A binding access constraint therefore acts as an implicit child tax
$\kappa\zeta_i^m$.

\section{Efficiency and Fertility}

The planner comparison holds the stationary cross-section, entry probabilities,
tenure assignments, savings, and real size-limit constraints fixed at the
instant of the variation. The planner counts true household preferences and
true resource costs. It does not count the avoided capital-gains tax as a social
benefit.

\begin{proposition}[Local young-old housing misallocation]
Consider a stationary equilibrium. Hold entry, tenure assignments, savings, and
real size-limit constraints fixed. Take a young owner whose owner size limit is
slack and whose financing constraint binds, so $\zeta_i^{O,F}>0$, and an old
incumbent at an interior retention choice. If one unit of existing housing can
be reassigned from the old incumbent to the young owner at zero real
implementation cost, the planner's goods-equivalent surplus is
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{(q^O+\zeta_i^{O,F})}_{\text{young buyer's marginal value}}
-
\underbrace{(q-\bar\ell)}_{\text{old incumbent's marginal value}}
=
\zeta_i^{O,F}+\frac{\bar\ell}{1+r}+\bar\ell.
\label{eq:compact_efficiency_gap}
\end{equation}
If this expression is positive, the competitive allocation does not solve the
planner's conditional allocation problem.
\end{proposition}

The market price cancels. What remains are the young household's financing gap,
the buyer's anticipated gains tax, and the old incumbent's realized gains tax.
The two tax terms are transfers, not resource costs when fiscal revenue is
returned lump sum. If implementing the
reassignment requires real cost $r_i^F$ per unit, that cost subtracts from the
surplus. If the down-payment constraint is a primitive technological
enforcement constraint and no instrument can relax it, the variation is not
feasible.

Old occupancy is not inefficient by itself. An old owner who keeps a large
house because she values it, or because she values leaving bequests, is using
housing for reasons the planner also counts. The inefficiency is the part of
retention supported by the forgiveness of capital-gains taxes at death, paired
with young households who value family-sized space above its market user cost.

The same cap also determines fertility. Fix a tenure mode and a household whose
housing is pinned at the effective family-housing cap $H$. For this comparison,
suppose saving can be summarized by a constant load $k\ge0$ on residual adult
consumption:
\[
(1+k)(c-\chi n)+\chi n+q^mH=w,
\qquad w=y+a.
\]
The log-bequest case $\Bcal(e)=b\log e$ gives
$k=\beta(1+\gamma+b)$; with no bequest motive, set $b=0$. After substituting
this saving rule into the budget, fertility along the binding cap solves
\[
\frac{\vartheta}{n}
=
\frac{(1+k)\chi}{w-q^mH-\chi n}
+
\frac{\alpha\kappa}{H-\kappa n},
\]
Differentiating with respect to the cap gives
\begin{equation}
\frac{\dd n}{\dd H}
=
\frac{
\dfrac{\alpha\kappa}{(H-\kappa n)^2}
-
\dfrac{(1+k)\chi q^m}{(w-q^mH-\chi n)^2}
}{
\dfrac{\vartheta}{n^2}
+\dfrac{(1+k)\chi^2}{(w-q^mH-\chi n)^2}
+\dfrac{\alpha\kappa^2}{(H-\kappa n)^2}
}.
\label{eq:compact_fertility_cap_derivative}
\end{equation}
Relaxing access raises fertility when the space-relief force dominates the
budget-tightening force. A useful sufficient condition along every binding cap
is
\begin{equation}
    \chi\le \frac{(1+k)\kappa q^m}{\alpha}.
\label{eq:compact_equal_scaling}
\end{equation}
The derivative connects the misallocation wedge to fertility: policies that
raise the effective family-housing cap matter only for households whose binding
constraint they actually relax.

This household derivative aggregates into a local sufficient statistic for
births. Let $z$ be a policy parameter. For renters,
$H_i^R=\bar h^R$; for owners,
\[
H_i^O(q)
=
\min\left\{
\bar h^O,
\frac{a_i\rho}{(1-\phi)q}
\right\}.
\]
Let $\mathcal E_m(z)$ be the set of types for whom tenure mode $m$ is the
unique active tenure, the effective cap $H_i^m$ is strictly binding, and the
binding component of the cap is locally unique. The pass-through of policy $z$
to the effective family-housing cap is
\[
\mathcal P_i^m(z)=\frac{\partial H_i^m(z)}{\partial z}.
\]
For an exposed type, write residual adult consumption and residual space as
\[
x_i^m=c_i^m-\chi n_i^m,
\qquad
s_i^m=h_i^m-\kappa n_i^m.
\]
The fixed-mode fertility response to one more unit of permitted family housing
is
\begin{equation}
\Psi_i^m
=
\frac{
\dfrac{\alpha\kappa}{(s_i^m)^2}
-
\dfrac{\chi q^m}{(1+k)(x_i^m)^2}
}{
\dfrac{\vartheta}{(n_i^m)^2}
+
\dfrac{\chi^2}{(1+k)(x_i^m)^2}
+
\dfrac{\alpha\kappa^2}{(s_i^m)^2}
}.
\label{eq:compact_psi}
\end{equation}
The value of relaxing the cap in utility units is
\begin{equation}
\Theta_i^m
\equiv
\frac{\partial W_i^m}{\partial H_i^m}
=
\frac{\alpha}{s_i^m}-\frac{q^m}{x_i^m}
=
\frac{\zeta_i^m}{x_i^m}.
\label{eq:compact_theta_cap}
\end{equation}
Thus the same access gap that measures misallocation also governs the entry
response.

\begin{proposition}[Local fixed-state fertility statistic]
\label{prop:local_fertility_stat}
Holding prices, tenure choices, active sets, outside fertility, and the
stationary demographic state fixed, the local effect of a cap-shifting policy
on cohort births is
\begin{equation}
\left.\frac{\dd N}{\dd z}\right|_{q,\mathrm{tenure},\mathcal A}
=
\bar M
\sum_{m\in\{R,O\}}
\int_{\mathcal E_m(z)}
\left[
\pi_i^E\Psi_i^m
+
(n_i^m-\bar n_i^E)
\frac{\pi_i^E(1-\pi_i^E)}{\kappa_E}
\Theta_i^m
\right]
\mathcal P_i^m(z)\,\dd G_Y(i).
\label{eq:compact_local_fertility_stat}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

The first term is the intensive margin: already-entered households change
completed fertility by $\Psi_i^m$ for each unit of cap relief. The second term
is the entry margin: cap relief raises the value of entering, and marginal
entrants bring their inside fertility net of the fertility they would have had
outside the modeled market. Both terms are gated by the same exposure set. A
policy aimed at a slack constraint has zero pass-through in this channel.

At one benchmark point the fertility response and the access gap are the same
object.

\begin{corollary}[Equal scaling: one statistic for both margins]
\label{cor:equal_scaling}
Suppose $\chi=(1+k)\kappa q^m/\alpha$, equal scaling at lifetime prices in
mode $m$, and the effective cap binds strictly. Then
\begin{equation}
\Psi_i^m
=
\frac{\kappa}{\alpha}\,
\frac{\zeta_i^m}{x_i^m}\,
\omega_i^m,
\qquad
\omega_i^m
=
\frac{
\dfrac{\alpha}{s_i^m}+\dfrac{q^m}{x_i^m}
}{
\dfrac{\vartheta}{(n_i^m)^2}
+\dfrac{\chi^2}{(1+k)(x_i^m)^2}
+\dfrac{\alpha\kappa^2}{(s_i^m)^2}
}
>0.
\label{eq:compact_equal_scaling_psi}
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

Under equal scaling, the normalized access gap $\zeta_i^m/x_i^m$ is
simultaneously the misallocation wedge of the efficiency proposition, the
fertility exposure $\Psi_i^m$ up to the positive weight $\omega_i^m$, and
the entry response $\Theta_i^m$. This is the object the quantitative model
measures: Part II reports the distribution of $\zeta$ among fertile
households and the share of the fertility-response mass that sits on
$\zeta>0$ types.

One pass-through has a closed form that Part II uses. For a
collateral-capped owner, $H_i^O=a_i\rho/[(1-\phi)q]$ with $\rho=r+\delta+\tau^p$.
Holding the user cost and the old-owner gains wedge fixed, a property-tax
increase raises $\rho$, lowers the
asset price $P=q/\rho$, and thereby lowers the down payment per unit of house:
\begin{equation}
\left.\frac{\partial H_i^O}{\partial\tau^p}\right|_{q,\bar\ell}
=
\frac{a_i}{(1-\phi)q}
>0.
\label{eq:compact_capitalization_passthrough}
\end{equation}
A property tax, capitalized into the asset price, relaxes the collateral
cap by $a_i/[(1-\phi)q]$ units per point of tax, and the birth response
follows from Proposition~\ref{prop:local_fertility_stat} integrated over
the collateral-exposed set. Whether the relief creates births
in practice depends on whether exposed households are near their fertility
margin when the relief arrives. Part II shows that timing, not exposure, is
the binding failure.

The formula is fixed-tenure accounting. If tenure choice is deterministic and
the type distribution has density near the rent-own boundary, a policy can also
move households across that boundary. Let
\[
\Delta_i(z)=W_i^O(z)-W_i^R(z),
\qquad
\mathcal I(z)=\{i:\Delta_i(z)=0\},
\]
and suppose $\nabla_i\Delta_i(z)\ne0$ on $\mathcal I(z)$, with unique mode
optima and no mass points at kinks. The tenure-switching component is
\begin{equation}
\left.\frac{\dd N^{\mathrm{switch}}}{\dd z}\right|_{q,\mathcal A}
=
\bar M
\int_{\mathcal I(z)}
\pi_i^E
\left(n_i^O-n_i^R\right)
\frac{\partial_z\Delta_i(z)}
{\|\nabla_i\Delta_i(z)\|}
f(i)\,\dd S(i),
\label{eq:compact_switching_births}
\end{equation}
where $f(i)$ is the type density and $\dd S(i)$ is unsigned surface measure on
the indifference set. The normal $\nabla_i\Delta_i/\|\nabla_i\Delta_i\|$ is
oriented toward the owner region $\{i:\Delta_i>0\}$. The total fixed-price effect is
\begin{equation}
\left.\frac{\dd N}{\dd z}\right|_q
=
\left.\frac{\dd N}{\dd z}\right|_{q,\mathrm{tenure},\mathcal A}
+
\left.\frac{\dd N^{\mathrm{switch}}}{\dd z}\right|_{q,\mathcal A}.
\label{eq:compact_total_fixed_price_births}
\end{equation}
There is no separate entry-boundary term under logit entry because the inside
value is continuous at the tenure boundary. The discontinuity is in conditional
fertility, $\pi_i^E(n_i^O-n_i^R)$. If $q^O=q^R$, the two tenure modes differ
only through caps and the boundary fertility jump disappears when the boundary
allocation is the same in both modes. With a tax wedge, ownership is the more
expensive way to access family space; the switching term is first order, but
its sign depends on the fertility jump of boundary households.

The direction of movement across the tenure boundary does not by itself sign
the birth response. The integrand also contains $n_i^O-n_i^R$. When ownership
has the higher effective price, the cheaper tenure is constrained at the
boundary, the price gap dominates any continuation-value difference, and the
equal-scaling condition holds, the family-size jump is positive only if the
within-cap space force also dominates the continuation-value level shift. This
is the integral condition derived in the full theory note.
Under those sufficient conditions an owner-access policy has a nonnegative
switching component, while relaxing the rental cap has a nonpositive one.
Outside those conditions, either sign is possible.

\part{Quantitative Lifecycle Model}
% ==========================================================

\section{Purpose and Timing}

The quantitative model embeds the same mechanism in a finite-life economy. It
has the standard lifecycle structure: households age, face income risk, save,
choose whether to rent or own, choose housing size, decide fertility, and
eventually leave bequests. The housing market has one aggregate user cost and
one asset price. Tenure matters because it changes the size menu, financing
constraints, transaction costs, and tax exposure.

Time is discrete. Households enter adult life at age $a=1$, retire after age
$J_R$, and die at the end of age $J$. The individual state at the beginning of a
period is
\begin{equation}
x=(b,z,d,a,n,s).
\label{eq:quant_state}
\end{equation}
Here $b$ is liquid wealth, $z\in\Zset$ is idiosyncratic productivity, $d$ is
tenure and housing position, $a$ is age, $n$ is an encoded completed-family-size
state, and $s$ is the child-at-home state. Tenure belongs to
\begin{equation}
\Dset=\{R,O_1,\ldots,O_K\}.
\label{eq:quant_tenure_set}
\end{equation}
$R$ denotes renting. $O_k$ denotes ownership of a house with physical size
$H_k$, where $H_1<\cdots<H_K$. Renters choose housing continuously up to
$\bar h^R$; owners choose from the discrete ladder. The ladder is the
quantitative version of the family-housing segmentation in Part I.

Income for working households is
\begin{equation}
y(a,z)=(1-\tau_{\mathrm{pay}})w e_a z,
\qquad
z'\sim \Pi^z(\cdot|z),
\qquad
a\le J_R,
\label{eq:quant_income}
\end{equation}
and retired households receive pension income $p^{\mathrm{ret}}$. The wage is
common apart from age and productivity because the quantitative mechanism is
housing access, tenure, and lifecycle wealth.

\section{Preferences and Bequests}

Let $m(n,s)$ be the number of dependent children living at home. Encoded
completed family size $n$ and current dependents differ because children
eventually mature.
Household needs are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bar c(n,s)&=\bar c_0+\bar c_n m(n,s),\\
\bar h(n,s)&=\bar h_0
+\bar h_{\mathrm{jump}}\1\{m(n,s)\ge1\}
+\bar h_n m(n,s).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:quant_child_needs}
\end{equation}
The first child can create a discrete housing-space jump; additional dependent
children raise the required space linearly.

Flow utility is Stone--Geary over consumption and usable housing space, wrapped
in CRRA. The owner service premium applies after household needs are removed:
\begin{equation}
u(c,h,d;n,s)
=
\frac{
\left[
(c-\bar c(n,s))^{\alpha_c}
\mathfrak h(h,d;n,s)^{1-\alpha_c}
\right]^{1-\sigma}
}{1-\sigma}
+\psi_{\mathrm{child}}m(n,s),
\label{eq:quant_flow_utility}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\mathfrak h(h,d;n,s)=
\begin{cases}
h^R-\bar h(n,s), & d=R,\\
\chi_O\big[H_k-\bar h(n,s)\big], & d=O_k,
\end{cases}
\qquad
\chi_O>0.
\label{eq:quant_housing_services}
\end{equation}
Utility is $-\infty$ when residual consumption or usable space is nonpositive.
In market clearing, an owner demands the physical quantity $H_k$, not the
preference-scaled quantity $\chi_O[H_k-\bar h(n,s)]$.

At death, households receive warm-glow bequest utility. The house passes to
the estate at market value: there is no forced sale, no transaction cost,
and no tax at death, so bequeathable wealth is $W^B=b+PH(d)$, liquid wealth
plus the gross value of any house held. Bequest utility is
\begin{equation}
\Bcal(W^B,n)
=
\theta_0(1+\theta_n n)
\frac{
(\theta_1+\max\{W^B,0\})^{1-\sigma}
}{1-\sigma}.
\label{eq:quant_bequest}
\end{equation}
This utility is not normalized to zero at a zero estate. Because its scale
depends on $n$, that level is part of the family-size choice. The parameter
$\theta_n$ lets completed fertility affect the desired estate even after
children have left the household.

\section{Housing, Finance, and Taxes}

There is one aggregate housing-services market. The user cost $q$ and asset
price $P$ satisfy
\begin{equation}
q=(r+\delta+\tau^p)P.
\label{eq:quant_user_cost}
\end{equation}
Housing supply is upward sloping:
\begin{equation}
H^S(q)=H_0\left(\frac{q}{\bar q}\right)^\eta.
\label{eq:quant_supply}
\end{equation}
The fixed-stock compact model is the limiting case $\eta=0$ around the
benchmark stock.

Renters choose $c,h^R,b'$ subject to
\begin{equation}
c+q h^R+b'=R b+y(a,z),
\qquad
0\le h^R\le \bar h^R,
\qquad
b'\ge0,
\label{eq:quant_renter_budget}
\end{equation}
where $R=1+r$. Owners of rung $H_k$ choose $c,b'$ subject to the owner branch
budget and the borrowing limit described below. Owners pay maintenance and
property taxes through $(\delta+\tau^p)PH_k$.

At origination, a buyer can finance at most a share $\phi$ of house value, so the
required down payment is
\begin{equation}
    (1-\phi)PH_k.
\label{eq:quant_dp}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, adjusted liquid wealth immediately after purchase must satisfy
$\tilde b\ge-\phi PH_k$. End-of-period saving for every owner, whether a buyer
or an incumbent, faces the same debt floor:
\begin{equation}
b'\ge -\phi P H_k.
\label{eq:quant_ltv}
\end{equation}

Housing adjustment is costly. A sale of house $H_k$ incurs the proportional
transaction cost $\psi^s$, so net sale proceeds are
\begin{equation}
\Omega(H_k)
=(1-\psi^s)PH_k.
\label{eq:quant_sale_proceeds}
\end{equation}
Transaction costs are the only disposal friction in the computed benchmark:
an owner who sells pays $\psi^s$ per unit of value and faces no tax on
accrued gains.

The capital-gains treatment of Part I is specified as an extension and is
not part of the computed benchmark. Pricing realized gains requires adding a
tax basis $B^p$ to the owner's state; a sale would then trigger
$\Tcal^g(PH_k,B^p)=\tau^g\max\{PH_k-B^p-\bar g,0\}$ with statutory exclusion
$\bar g$, net proceeds would fall to $\Omega(H_k)-\Tcal^g(PH_k,B^p)$, and
stepped-up basis at death would forgive the accrued gain. The benchmark
state vector \eqref{eq:quant_state} deliberately excludes $B^p$: the
old-owner lock-in wedge $\bar\ell$ is priced analytically in Part I, and the
counterfactual section instead bounds its quantitative content with a direct
retention-margin experiment.

\section{Fertility and Child Aging}

Fertility is chosen during the fertile window
\[
a\in\{A_f^{\mathrm{start}},\ldots,A_f^{\mathrm{end}}\}.
\]
A childless household chooses
\begin{equation}
n'\in\Nset=\{0,1,\ldots,\bar n\}.
\label{eq:quant_fertility_set}
\end{equation}
The option $n'=0$ means no birth in the current period. A positive choice fixes
completed fertility forever and moves the household into the first
dependent-child state. A household that reaches the end of the fertile window
without a positive choice remains childless.

In the computation, $\Nset=\{0,1,2\}$ and the last state is the open-ended
highest family-size category. The completed-fertility moment matched to the CPS
is reported as $2\E[n]$. This household-to-woman conversion is applied only to
that reported moment. Child needs and bequest utility use the encoded state
$n$, while the housing-size target maps $n=1$ to the 1--2 group and $n=2$ to
the 3+ group. The distinction is a unit convention and does not create an
additional fertility choice.

Let $s=0$ denote no dependent children and let $s=1,\ldots,S_c$ denote
dependent-child stages. After a positive fertility choice, the household enters
$s=1$. Children age stochastically through the stages according to $\Pi^c$.
While children are at home, $m(n,s)=n$; after maturity, $m(n,s)=0$. Completed
fertility still matters through bequest utility.

\section{Household Problem}

Within the period, the household first faces fertility if eligible, then
chooses tenure and housing position, then chooses consumption, rental housing,
and saving. Let the continuation value after income risk and child aging be
\begin{equation}
\bar V_{a+1}(b',d',z,n,s)
=
\sum_{z'}\sum_{s'}
\Pi^z(z'|z)\Pi^c(s'|s,n)
V_{a+1}(b',z',d',n,s').
\label{eq:quant_continuation}
\end{equation}
The terminal value is
\[
V_{J+1}(b,z,d,n,s)=\Bcal(W^B,n).
\]

Conditional on renting, the branch value is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
V_a^R(b,z,n,s)
=
\max_{c,h^R,b'}\quad
&u(c,h^R,R;n,s)+\beta \bar V_{a+1}(b',R,z,n,s)\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
&c+q h^R+b'=Rb+y(a,z),\\
&0\le h^R\le\bar h^R,\qquad b'\ge0.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:quant_renter_problem}
\end{equation}
Conditional on owning rung $H_k$ after any adjustment, the branch value is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
V_a^{O_k}(\tilde b,z,n,s)
=
\max_{c,b'}\quad
&u(c,H_k,O_k;n,s)+\beta \bar V_{a+1}(b',O_k,z,n,s)\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
&c+(\delta+\tau^p)PH_k+b'=R\tilde b+y(a,z),\\
&b'\ge -\phi P H_k.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:quant_owner_problem}
\end{equation}
The debt floor is the same for a buyer and an incumbent. Purchase feasibility
is screened separately through the down-payment condition before this branch is
evaluated.

Let $H(R)=0$ and $H(O_k)=H_k$. The liquid wealth available after housing
adjustment is
\begin{equation}
\tilde b(b,d,d')
=
b
+\1\{d\in\Oset,\ d'\neq d\}\Omega(H(d))
-\1\{d'\in\Oset,\ d'\neq d\}PH(d').
\label{eq:quant_adjusted_wealth}
\end{equation}
A seller collects net proceeds, a buyer pays the full purchase price, and a
household that keeps its position pays nothing.

The tenure branch value for feasible $d'$ is
\begin{equation}
\widetilde V_a^T(d'|b,z,d,n,s)
=
\begin{cases}
V_a^R(\tilde b(b,d,R),z,n,s), & d'=R,\\
V_a^{O_k}(\tilde b(b,d,O_k),z,n,s), & d'=O_k.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:quant_tenure_branch}
\end{equation}
Tenure and owner-rung choices have Type-I extreme-value shocks with scale
$\kappa_T$:
\begin{equation}
\pi_a^T(d'|x)
=
\frac{\exp\{\widetilde V_a^T(d'|x)/\kappa_T\}}
{\sum_{\hat d\in\Dset(x)}
\exp\{\widetilde V_a^T(\hat d|x)/\kappa_T\}},
\label{eq:quant_tenure_probs}
\end{equation}
and the inclusive tenure value is
\begin{equation}
V_a^T(x)
=
\kappa_T\log
\sum_{\hat d\in\Dset(x)}
\exp\{\widetilde V_a^T(\hat d|x)/\kappa_T\}.
\label{eq:quant_tenure_iv}
\end{equation}
At the estimated value $\kappa_T=0$, these expressions mean their deterministic
limit: the branch with the highest value is chosen with probability one, and a
tie is resolved by the fixed ordering of feasible branches.

For a childless household in the fertile window, the value of fertility option
$n'$ is
\begin{equation}
\widetilde V_a^F(n'|b,z,d)
=
V_a^T(b,z,d,n',s(n')),
\qquad
s(n')=\1\{n'>0\}.
\label{eq:quant_fert_branch}
\end{equation}
With Type-I extreme-value shocks of scale $\kappa_F$,
\begin{equation}
\pi_a^F(n'|b,z,d)
=
\frac{\exp\{\widetilde V_a^F(n'|b,z,d)/\kappa_F\}}
{\sum_{\hat n\in\Nset}
\exp\{\widetilde V_a^F(\hat n|b,z,d)/\kappa_F\}}.
\label{eq:quant_fert_probs}
\end{equation}
The value before the fertility draw is the corresponding inclusive value:
\begin{equation}
V_a(b,z,d,0,0)
=
\kappa_F\log
\sum_{\hat n\in\Nset}
\exp\{\widetilde V_a^F(\hat n|b,z,d)/\kappa_F\}.
\label{eq:quant_fert_iv}
\end{equation}
Outside the fertile window, or after a positive fertility choice, this stage is
degenerate and $V_a=V_a^T$.

\section{Stationary Equilibrium}

Let $g^-$ be the stationary distribution at the beginning of a period, before
the current fertility, tenure, and housing choices. Applying those choices to
$g^-$ produces the post-decision distribution $g^+$. Flow utility, current
housing demand, and age-specific moments are measured with $g^+$; the law of
motion maps $g^+$ into next period's $g^-$. Adult mass is normalized to one:
\begin{equation}
    \int dg^-(x)=\int dg^+(x)=1.
\label{eq:quant_mass_norm}
\end{equation}
This normalization fixes the demographic envelope and lets fertility change
household composition, tenure, and housing demand. It is not, by itself, a
demographic closure for total births: the entry inflow of new adults is a
fixed constant and births do not feed it. Quantitative counterfactuals
therefore report per-capita objects --- completed fertility, childlessness,
tenure --- at fixed population. The entry and scale margin of Part I is
reinstated on top of the solved model by an outer fixed point described in
Section~\ref{sec:quant_entry_margin}, and its results are reported
separately from the fixed-population results.

\begin{definition}[Stationary equilibrium]
A stationary equilibrium is a user cost and asset price $(q,P)$, value
functions and policies, tenure and fertility probabilities, stationary
distributions $(g^-,g^+)$, and aggregate housing quantities $(H^D,H^S)$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item households solve the branch problems
\eqref{eq:quant_renter_problem}--\eqref{eq:quant_owner_problem}, tenure choice
is given by \eqref{eq:quant_tenure_probs}, fertility choice is given by
\eqref{eq:quant_fert_probs}, and terminal utility is \eqref{eq:quant_bequest};
\item current fertility and tenure policies map $g^-$ into $g^+$, and the
income transition, child aging, deterministic aging, death, and entry of new
adult households map $g^+$ back into $g^-$;
\item aggregate physical housing demand is
\begin{equation}
H^D
=
\int
\left[
\1\{d=R\}h^R(x)
+\sum_{k=1}^K\1\{d=O_k\}H_k
\right]\dd g^+(x);
\label{eq:quant_hd}
\end{equation}
\item the housing market clears and the user-cost relation holds:
\begin{equation}
H^D=H^S(q),
\qquad
q=(r+\delta+\tau^p)P;
\label{eq:quant_clearing}
\end{equation}
\item payroll taxes, property taxes, capital-gains taxes, pensions, and any
rebates satisfy the government accounting rule specified for the calibration or
counterfactual.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\section{Computation}
\label{sec:quant_computation}

One model period is four years. Period $j=0,\ldots,16$ begins at age
$18+4j$; the last interval covers ages 82--85. Retirement begins at 66, and
the last period in which a birth can occur begins at 42. Annual objects are
converted before solution:
\[
R=(1+r_{\mathrm{ann}})^4,
\quad
\beta=\beta_{\mathrm{ann}}^4,
\quad
\delta=1-(1-\delta_{\mathrm{ann}})^4,
\quad
\tau^p=4\tau^p_{\mathrm{ann}}.
\]
Income and consumption-flow quantities are expressed in four-year units;
housing remains in rooms.

The wealth grid has $N_b=120$ nodes on $[-35,100]$ with power spacing near
zero. Income takes values $z\in\{0.6,0.8,1.0,1.2,1.4\}$ with invariant
weights $\pi_z=(0.1,0.2,0.4,0.2,0.1)$ and transition matrix
$\Pi^z=0.85I+0.15\mathbf 1\pi_z'$. One dependent-child stage has an expected
duration of 18 years, giving a four-year maturity probability of $2/9$.
Entrants have mass $1/J$ each period. The balanced pension equals payroll-tax
revenue per retiree in the stationary age distribution.

Value functions are computed by backward induction. Golden-section search
solves saving in each branch, and Howard iteration accelerates evaluation.
The stationary distribution follows by forward iteration, while damped
fixed-point iteration and a scalar bracketing refinement solve the housing
market. The intended market-residual tolerance is $10^{-4}$; strict rejection
of candidates above that tolerance is part of the corrected production run.

Because tenure and fertility choices are close to deterministic at the
calibrated point, moments built on buy thresholds and the borrowing
constraint move with the wealth grid, and grid resolution matters more
here than in a standard lifecycle model. An earlier calibration at $N_b=60$
re-evaluated 18 percent worse at $N_b=120$ at the same parameters; part of
its fit was grid noise. The July snapshot was searched at $N_b=120$ and
re-evaluated at $N_b=240$. The corrected estimation will use the same search
and verification grids.

\label{sec:quant_calibration}

\subsection{Externally set objects}

Prices, finance, demography, and the housing menu are set outside the
moment-matching loop.

\section{The Mechanism Quantified}
\label{sec:quant_mechanism}

Part I's argument runs through one object, the access gap $\zeta$: the
amount by which a constrained household's marginal valuation of family
space exceeds its market user cost. Under equal scaling, the corollary to
Proposition~\ref{prop:local_fertility_stat} says the same gap prices
misallocation, fertility exposure, and entry. The July snapshot gives a first
measurement of that gap.

The measured gap is large. Among
fertile-age renting households pinned at the rental cap, the mean access
gap is 0.248 goods units, 1.28 times the market user cost, so capped
renters value a marginal room at 2.3 times what the market charges for it.
Weighting fertility responses by exposure as in
Proposition~\ref{prop:local_fertility_stat}, 73 percent of the model's
fertility-response mass sits on households with $\zeta>0$. Within the July
diagnostics, the share is similar across grid resolutions and owner menus.
Its level remains provisional because the exposure weights will change when
current housing choices are measured contemporaneously.

The model places many purchases near the fertility deadline. The purchase
region expands in the last fertile period, when the option value of waiting to
have children expires. Buyers in this region hold wealth above the required
down payment. In the current model, housing access therefore does not operate
only by forcing households to save toward a distant threshold. It also
compresses births and purchases into a joint decision near the deadline. The
corrected run will determine how much of this pattern survives contemporaneous
measurement.

\section{Preliminary Policy Patterns}
\label{sec:quant_policy}

The instruments follow the access margins of the compact model: a holding
tax on large owner units (the capitalization channel of
eq.~\ref{eq:compact_capitalization_passthrough}), birth-linked down-payment
grants restricted to family-sized rungs (the financing gap
$\zeta^{O,F}$), variation in the rental cap $\bar h^R$, a retention wedge
on old owners, and a revenue-linked package combining the tax and the
grant. These exercises describe the July diagnostic runtime, not final policy
estimates. The grant enters both household values and the forward distribution
in that runtime. Prices re-clear in every fixed-population experiment, but all
cases will be rerun after re-estimation.

The current tax experiment produces capitalization and a small birth response.
A two percent holding tax on large units lowers their asset price by 12
percent. Capitalization is similar across the grids, moment weights, and owner
menus that have been checked, while completed fertility rises by $+0.004$ to
$+0.009$. As eq.~\ref{eq:compact_capitalization_passthrough} predicts, down
payments fall. In this snapshot, exposed households buy near the fertility deadline with wealth
already above the threshold, so cheaper access changes which house they
buy more than whether they have children.

The grant runtime produces a dose--response. A down-payment grant paid at
a birth, restricted to family-sized rungs, raises completed fertility by
$+0.01$, $+0.13$, $+0.26$, and $+0.49$ at grant sizes 0.1, 0.4, 0.58, and
1.0, in units of mean annual income. In the current solution the access
instrument has a larger response because it is conditional on the birth itself:
it closes the financing gap at the moment the household is on its
fertility margin. The conditioning also distorts. Small grants delay
pre-birth purchases, since households near a birth wait for the subsidy,
and young ownership falls from 0.23 to 0.09 at the smallest grant. The
distortion fades at larger grant sizes, but any conditional instrument
inherits the margin.

The combined experiment follows both sides of Part I's reallocation.
The two percent tax on large homes funds the birth-linked grant; the tax
lowers incumbents' retention value and the price of large units by 11
percent, and the grant closes the young side's financing gap at the birth
margin. At the July calibration the package raises completed fertility by
$+0.15$ and is revenue-funded. This magnitude is provisional.

\subsection{The entry and scale margin}
\label{sec:quant_entry_margin}

The results above hold population fixed. The entry margin of Part I is
reinstated by an outer fixed point around the solved model: the policy
changes household values, values move the entry probability against the
outside option and the number of city-born future entrants, the implied
stationary population scale $S$ multiplies aggregate housing demand, and
the price re-clears against the supply curve. Households never see $S$
directly; population size operates through the price of space. The outside
value and entry scale are recovered at the benchmark so that the baseline
is exactly $S=1$. The recovered conventions are documented rather than
estimated, and the binding one is the assumption that city-born children
become future entrants one-for-one.

These results use $N_b=120$ at the July calibration. The fixed-population

\section{Current Repairs and Re-estimation}
\label{sec:quant_repairs}

The first repair concerns timing. The Bellman problem gives a household its
new tenure and house in the current period, but the July distribution code uses
inherited tenure for market clearing and several moments. The corrected
operator will form a post-decision distribution for current demand and moments,
while preserving the next-period distribution for state transitions. The same
change will add current purchases to the housing response measured at a first
birth.

The second repair covers the first earnings cell. The intended profile begins
at 0.65 of peak earnings at age 18, but the uncovered first cell in the July
runtime remained at one. The new profile will cover every age explicitly.
Neither correction has yet been re-estimated, and I do not infer the new fit
from their mechanical directions.

The production run also needs one recorded configuration for the five owner
rungs, parameter bounds, target definitions, and the 120/240 wealth grids. A
candidate will be ranked only if it meets the strict market-clearing tolerance.
The forward-map grant correction used for the July policy runtime will be
carried into the main package, and the entry and retention drivers will be
included in the same reproducible path. The quantitative sequence is therefore
fixed: verify the two timing identities, re-estimate at $N_b=120$, verify at
$N_b=240$, regenerate the diagnostic packet, and rerun the policy table.

The paper-side specification is already aligned with the estimated object in
three places. Owner utility applies $\chi_O$ to usable space
$H_k-\bar h(n,s)$, bequest utility retains its family-size-dependent level, and
all owners face the same debt floor. Those changes describe the existing
estimated preferences and finance rule; they are not new mechanisms.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:quant_limitations}

The model remains deliberately incomplete after these repairs. Old-age
ownership is too high because health, mortality, and other reasons for
downsizing are absent. Fertility has no age-dependent fecundity, so waiting is
costless until the final fertile period. Both omissions matter for the timing
and stock-allocation questions at the center of the paper.

The computed benchmark prices old-owner retention through transaction costs
only. The capital-gains and basis mechanism of Part I is not in the
quantitative state space. The supply curve is unit-elastic, while the compact
misallocation result holds the stock fixed. Finally, the entry exercise relies
on an externally recovered outside value and on the convention that local
births become future entrants. The entry table is therefore a decomposition,
not an estimated migration response.

\section{Conclusion}

Children use housing space, and access to that space differs sharply by
tenure. A rental-size limit raises the shadow price of room for a constrained
household. Ownership relaxes the size limit but introduces a down payment.
Retention frictions can keep large homes with older households whose current
use of space is low. The compact model places these margins in one
goods-equivalent access gap and shows how the same gap enters the private price
of children.

The July quantitative snapshot suggests that this mechanism is active. Many
households near the fertility margin value additional space above its user
cost, purchases bunch late in the fertile window, and the data show that older
households hold most family-sized owner housing. The size of these effects is
not yet settled. Current tenure timing and the first earnings cell affect
targeted moments and require a new calibration.

The immediate goal is therefore narrower than a new model extension: align the
distribution with the household timing, restore the intended income profile,
freeze one production configuration, and re-estimate. The theory and the data
fact organize that exercise. The corrected fit and policy table will determine
how much quantitative weight the mechanism can carry.
