Family Space and the Urban Fertility Gradient

A figure read of the current ACS/MMS and AHS evidence. The organizing object is family-space access: buying, renting, and moving are access technologies, not primitive mechanisms by themselves.

The current evidence is strongest on the physical housing menu. Parents are less central in metros where the center is more tilted toward small units relative to family-capable units. The current renter-price premium evidence is weaker, especially in the bedroom construction.

1. The Aggregate Unit Menu

Couillard makes unit size central: fertility is more connected to the cost of family-sized units than to generic housing costs. The AHS snapshot adds a stock-menu fact. The United States has many 3+ bedroom units, so the claim cannot be that family-sized units literally do not exist. The sharper claim is that access depends on tenure, structure, and location.

2. The Within-Metro Unit Menu

The ACS/MMS bedroom packet uses Couillard-style bins: 0-1, 2, and 3+ bedrooms. The central stock is less family-sized than the periphery. This is the visual counterpart to the stock-scarcity measure used in the metro scatter below.

3. Parents And Fertility Are Less Central Where Central Family Space Is Scarce

Define the parent centrality gap as childless center share minus parent center share within a metro. Define central family-space stock scarcity as the central small/family stock ratio relative to the periphery. This is the main immediate empirical bridge from Couillard to the paper. The fertility counterpart uses periphery minus center fertility by metro, so positive values mean fertility is more peripheral.

The unconditional location levels are already meaningfully different: recent-birth rates are 6.3 percent in the center and 6.8 percent in the periphery; parent-with-child-under-18 rates are 45.1 percent in the center and 54.3 percent in the periphery; mean own children are 0.957 in the center and 1.149 in the periphery.

The level regressions separate two questions. In centers, recent-birth rates fall with central 3+ bedroom stock scarcity: slope -0.0058, SE 0.0034, p=0.092. In peripheries, the slope is positive but not significant: 0.0028, SE 0.0031, p=0.367. At the metro-total level, the slope is close to zero: -0.0007, SE 0.0028, p=0.800. So this cross-section supports a spatial reallocation pattern more clearly than a general metro-wide fertility decline.

4. Price Premia Are Weaker In This Pass

The current ACS/IPUMS renter-price construction does not yet give a clean Couillard-style rent-gradient result. In the bedroom packet, the parent centrality gap is basically flat in the central 3+ bedroom rent premium. This does not kill the mechanism, but it changes the empirical diagnosis: the current evidence is more about the physical access menu than about a clean positive observed rental premium.

5. What We Know About Supply Response

The first descriptive stock-growth exercise compares early 2012-2014 to late 2021-2023. It does not identify a causal supply elasticity. It is best read as a placeholder that says the next empirical work needs construction, vintage, land constraints, or zoning/regulation shifters.

6. Income And Sorting Context

The income cross-section is useful, but it should not be the headline. The paper needs income because family-space access depends on resources and because high-income childless households sort centrally. The central object remains family-space access, with income shaping who can buy, rent, stay, or move.

Bottom Line

  • Fact 1: births raise housing demand in the PSID no-location packet.
  • Fact 2: central housing is more small-unit tilted than peripheral housing.
  • Fact 3: parents and fertility are less central where central family-capable stock is relatively scarce.
  • Fact 4: ownership is empirically central, but as an access technology rather than the primitive mechanism.
  • Missing: causal supply evidence and location-specific PSID dynamics.
