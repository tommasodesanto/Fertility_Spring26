# Couillard And The AHS Family-Unit Supply Snapshot

This note links Couillard's `Build, Baby, Build` mechanism to the current AHS
2023 housing-stock snapshot. The local Couillard PDF and extracted text are in
`docs/literature/couillard/` for local reading.

## Couillard's Empirical Object

Couillard's core empirical distinction is not generic housing affordability
versus fertility. It is unit size. The reduced-form object is tract-level
fertility regressed on local median rents for different bedroom counts,
especially 1-bedroom versus 3+ bedroom units.

His Table 1 estimates

$$
GFR_{jt} = \alpha_{\mathrm{FE}(j),t} + \beta \log r^b_{jt} + \epsilon_{jt},
\qquad b \in \{1,3+\}.
$$

Across state-year, city-year, county-year, and tract-year fixed effects, the
3+ bedroom rent coefficient is negative and larger in magnitude than the
1-bedroom rent coefficient. In the first three specifications, the 3+ bedroom
coefficient is roughly 1.6 to 1.9 times the 1-bedroom coefficient; with tract
fixed effects it is roughly 3.6 times larger. Couillard interprets this as
evidence that family-sized housing costs are more fertility-relevant than the
price of small childless units.

He is careful that the reduced form is not clean causal fertility evidence by
itself. Local rents and local fertility are both shaped by endogenous sorting:
families can move away from high-cost places, and childless households can sort
into small-unit high-amenity places. This is why the paper moves from
bedroom-specific rent regressions to a structural neighborhood and unit-size
choice model.

The structural quantitative claims are:

- Holding non-housing fertility determinants fixed, rising rents since 1990
  account for about 13 million fewer births from 1990 to 2020.
- This accounts for roughly 11 percent of births over the period and about
  51 percent of the 2000s-to-2010s fertility decline.
- Average rent growth explains more than changes in relative rents, but a
  policy that shifts the supply of 3+ bedroom units has much larger fertility
  effects than an equal-cost small-unit supply policy.
- The large-unit policy produces about 4.7 million additional births over
  three decades, about 2.3 times the effect of the small-unit policy.

## What Our AHS Snapshot Adds

Couillard shows that 3+ bedroom rents are the empirically relevant housing-cost
margin. The AHS snapshot asks a complementary supply-menu question:

> How many family-capable units exist, and where are they located in the
> tenure/structure menu?

The current AHS packet is generated by
`code/data/ahs_supply_snapshot/analyze_ahs_family_unit_menu.R`.

Main local outputs:

- `code/data/ahs_supply_snapshot/output_ahs_family_unit_menu_national/AHS_2023_FAMILY_UNIT_MENU.md`
- `code/data/ahs_supply_snapshot/output_ahs_family_unit_menu_national/AHS_2023_FAMILY_UNIT_FIGURE_PACKET.pdf`
- `code/data/ahs_supply_snapshot/output_ahs_family_unit_menu_national/ahs_absolute_unit_counts.csv`

The national 2023 AHS count says the United States has about 145.3 million
housing units in the PUF. The bedroom menu is:

| Unit type | Count |
|---|---:|
| 0-1 bedrooms | 18.2 million |
| 2 bedrooms | 36.4 million |
| 3 bedrooms | 56.5 million |
| 4+ bedrooms | 34.3 million |
| 3+ bedrooms total | 90.7 million |

So the missing-family-housing claim cannot be "there are no large units" in the
aggregate stock. There are many 3+ bedroom units. The economically sharper fact
is that family-capable units are unevenly located in the stock menu:

| Object | Count |
|---|---:|
| 3+ bedroom units, owner | 70.6 million |
| 3+ bedroom units, renter | 13.4 million |
| 3+ bedroom units, vacant | 4.4 million |
| Missing-middle proxy units | 22.0 million |
| Missing-middle proxy units, renter | 13.9 million |
| Renter households with children in 0-2 bedroom units | 5.8 million |
| Owner households with children in 0-2 bedroom units | 1.8 million |

This makes tenure useful, but not as the only story. Tenure is diagnostic: it
tells us whether family-capable units are accessed by buying, renting, or
moving into different structure types. The point is not that the missing object
must be rental housing. The point is that the child-contingent family-space
margin is often mediated by a purchase/ownership transition.

## Economic Interpretation

The Couillard object is the family-size rent gradient:

$$
\log r^{3+}_{jt} - \log r^{1}_{jt}.
$$

The AHS object is the family-unit menu:

$$
Q_{s,T,k},
\qquad
s \in \{0\!-\!1,2,3,4+\}\text{ bedrooms},\;
T \in \{\mathrm{rent},\mathrm{own},\mathrm{vacant}\},\;
k \in \{\mathrm{detached},\mathrm{attached},\mathrm{multifamily}\}.
$$

The synthesis is:

1. Couillard documents that 3+ bedroom rents are more strongly associated with
   fertility than 1-bedroom rents.
2. The structural model says large-unit supply has high fertility returns
   because it relaxes the child-contingent housing cost.
3. AHS shows that 3+ bedroom units exist in large absolute numbers, but are
   disproportionately located in owner and detached segments.
4. Therefore the "missing middle" should be framed as missing accessible
   family-capable units: units that young households can reach through buying,
   renting, or moving within the metro, not simply the absence of any large
   housing units.

For our paper, the best empirical bridge is:

> Couillard shows that the fertility-relevant price is the price of
> family-sized housing. We show that the fertility-relevant supply object is
> not total housing and not even total 3+ bedroom housing, but the availability
> of family-capable units across the owner/renter and detached/moderate-density
> access margins.

## What To Build Next

The ACS/MMS bedroom packet now puts the Couillard size object next to the
within-metro sorting object:

- `code/data/mms_center_periphery/output_couillard_bedroom_supply/ACS_MMS_COUILLARD_BEDROOM_SUPPLY_PACKET.md`
- `code/data/mms_center_periphery/output_couillard_bedroom_supply/acs_bedroom_menu_center_periphery.png`
- `code/data/mms_center_periphery/output_couillard_bedroom_supply/acs_bedroom_parent_gap_vs_stock_scarcity.png`

The next empirical figures should sharpen these objects rather than just
replicate them:

1. Couillard-style bedroom rent gradients by metro or tract: 1-bedroom versus
   3+ bedroom rents, preferably from Census summary tables or finer geography
   because the ACS/IPUMS renter-price premium is flat in the current pass.
2. AHS/ACS stock-growth counts by bedroom bin: absolute number of 0-1, 2, 3,
   and 4+ bedroom units by vintage or construction period.
3. AHS accessible-family-stock counts: 3+ bedroom owner/renter units and
   2-3 bedroom attached/multifamily units.
4. A metro scatter: 3+ bedroom rent gradient on the vertical axis and
   family-sized stock scarcity on the horizontal axis.
5. A child-status stock-use figure: households with children by bedroom count
   and tenure, especially the count of renter households with children in
   0-2 bedroom units.

Those objects are more aligned with Couillard than a generic tenure story.
Tenure matters because it reveals the access technology for family space.
Ownership is central if the data show that buying is the main route into
family-sized housing; it should not be imposed as the primitive mechanism before
the stock, price, and event-study facts are read together.
