## Problem To Fix

The current draft has two generations, entry, old retention, bequests and
inheritances, and old housing endowments \(H_j^0\). But the competitive
equilibrium currently treats the following as exogenous cross-sectional
objects:

- young type distribution \(G_Y\),
- old incumbent distribution \(F_O\),
- old housing endowments \(H_j^0\),
- young inheritance exposure \(B_i\).

That is not a true stationary OLG equilibrium. In a stationary two-period OLG
model, today’s young choices generate tomorrow’s old incumbents, old housing
endowments, protected assessments, and inheritance exposure. Entry and exit
must be part of the stationary law of motion.

The current model is therefore closer to a static cross-sectional allocation
given old initial conditions. I want the analytical Part I to be a true
stationary OLG model.

## Required Model Changes

### 1. Add Calendar And Stationarity

Describe the economy as a stationary two-period OLG economy. Each period:

- a mass \(\bar M\) of potential young households is drawn,
- young households decide whether to enter the housing market,
- entered young choose tenure, housing, and fertility,
- old incumbents from the previous period decide how much housing to retain and
  how much bequest expenditure to make,
- old households exit after the retention/bequest decision,
- current young owners become next period’s old incumbents.

The timing should be stated once in the environment. Avoid long meta prose.

### 2. Replace Initial-Condition Language

\(H_j^0\) should not be an arbitrary initial condition in equilibrium. It should
be part of the stationary old-incumbent distribution generated by previous
young owner choices.

Use language like:

- “old incumbents are last period’s young owners,”
- “\(H_j^0\) is the housing carried into old age,”
- “in stationarity, the distribution of old incumbents is reproduced by the
  previous period’s owner choices.”

### 3. Define The Old-Incumbent Transition

Add a transition law from current young owners into next period old incumbents.
It can be reduced form, but it must be explicit.

Possible notation:

\[
\Gamma_O(\mathrm dj' \mid i,o,h,n)
\]

or a deterministic map. It should generate old state variables such as

\[
j'=(m_{j'},H_{j'}^0,\tilde P_{j'},\tilde\tau_{j'},\mathcal B_{j'}).
\]

The natural baseline can set \(H_{j'}^0=h_i^O\) for young owners. If the model
keeps only old incumbent owners, say explicitly that young renters and
nonentrants do not enter the old-retention block; they are outside the modeled
incumbent-owner margin. Do not leave this implicit.

### 4. Define The Young-Type / Inheritance Transition

\[

\[

must state that the stationary young distribution, including \(B_i\), is

If you choose to keep \(G_Y\) partly exogenous, say exactly which part is

### 5. Handle The Assessment Discount In Stationarity

This is important. In a literal steady state with constant \(P\) and no price
growth, a protected assessment inherited from purchase at the same \(P\) may
not generate \(L_j^\tau>0\). If the paper wants

\[
L_j^\tau(q,\tau^p)=\tau^pP-\tilde\tau_j\tilde P_j>0

in a stationary equilibrium, the model needs a stationary assessment rule that
can generate protected bases below current market tax liability.

Use a simple reduced-form policy rule. Options:

- a protected-assessment factor,
- an age/incumbency discount,
- a stationary kernel for \((\tilde P_j,\tilde\tau_j)\),
- or a rule mapping young owners into old protected assessments.

Do not leave \(L_j^\tau\) as a hidden historical initial condition if the
equilibrium is called stationary.
