A discrete-time, two-location, finite-horizon lifecycle of housing tenure and fertility — the math, the code, and one fully solved instance, in one page.

This page solves a discrete-time lifecycle model with two locations, four parities, endogenous tenure and fertility — and shows you the math, the Python that implements it, and the actual policies and prices that come out of one solve. Read it top-to-bottom, or jump from the right.

1. The economic question

Across high-income countries, two things have been moving in lockstep. Housing has been getting more expensive in centers — partly through inelastic supply (Gyourko, Mayer & Sinai 2013), partly through demand from skill-sorting and amenity capitalization. Fertility has been falling, with the largest drops in dense, expensive cities and at the margin of first births.

The traditional explanations are well-known. The quantity–quality tradeoff (Becker & Lewis 1973) says that as the price of child-quality inputs rises, parents shift toward fewer children. Sommer, Sullivan & Verbrugge (2013) point at the durable-goods nature of housing: kids are space-intensive, so housing prices are an effective tax on family size. Doepke & Kindermann (2019) show a large urban–rural fertility gap that has widened over the last three decades, and emphasize that policy responses to low fertility need to take spatial sorting seriously.

This project asks: how much of the cross-sectional and lifecycle variation in fertility, ownership, and location is explained by housing costs alone, once we let households endogenously choose where to live, whether to own, and how many children to have? The model below is a discrete-time, finite-horizon lifecycle. Two locations (Center and Periphery), an owner-housing ladder of fixed sizes, and one-shot completed-fertility decisions resolved at fertile ages. The remainder of the page is a guided tour.

What's not in the model. No endogenous wages, no marriage market, no human-capital investment for children, no rental supply curve. The point is to push hard on housing × fertility through a clean lifecycle, not to be everything to everyone.

2.2 Period utility

Renters consume Cobb–Douglas in $(c, h)$ with weight $\alpha$ on consumption; owners consume $c$ and receive fixed housing services $\chi \cdot H_{\mathrm{own}}[\mathrm{ten}-1]$. Both have child commitment floors that grow in parity: a baseline floor $(\bar c_0, \bar h_0)$, a per-child increment $(\bar c_n, \bar h_n)$, and a one-time discrete jump $\bar h_{\mathrm{jump}}$ when going from no children to any. Period utility is CRRA with curvature $\sigma$:

$$u(c, h, n) = \frac{\big[(c - \bar c_0 - \bar c_n n)^\alpha\,(h - \bar h_0 - \bar h_n n - \bar h_{\mathrm{jump}}\mathbf{1}[n\geq 1])^{1-\alpha}\big]^{1-\sigma} - 1}{1-\sigma}.$$ src: latex/Analytical_Notes.tex; impl: dt_cp_model/kernels.py:eval_renter_scalar

2.3 Bellman recursion

The recursion runs backward in age. At each age $j$ the period value is built in four stages — a deliberate decomposition that matches the kernel layout in solver.py:

  1. Conditional savings + tenure-conditional value. For each $(i, \mathrm{ten})$, choose $b'$ to maximize per-period plus continuation. Renters use golden-section search subject to the renter housing cap $h \leq h_R^{\max}$; owners' housing is fixed at $H_{\mathrm{own}}[\mathrm{ten}-1]$ and the only choice is $b'$. $$V_d(b, \mathrm{ten}, i, n, \mathrm{cs}, j) = \max_{b'} u(c, h, n) + \beta\, \mathbb{E}_{\mathrm{cs}'\,\mid\,\mathrm{cs},n}\!\big[V(b', \mathrm{ten}, i, n, \mathrm{cs}', j+1)\big].$$
  2. Tenure choice. Discrete max over destination tenures $t_n$, subject to wealth feasibility: $$V_H(b, t_o, i, n, \mathrm{cs}, j) = \max_{t_n \in \mathcal F(b, t_o, i)} V_d(b^*, t_n, i, n, \mathrm{cs}, j).$$
  3. Location logit. $$V_I(b, t_o, i, n, \mathrm{cs}, j) = \kappa_{\mathrm{loc}} \log \sum_{i' \in \{1,\ldots,I\}} \exp\!\Big(\tfrac{V_H(b_{i'}, t_o, i', n, \mathrm{cs}, j) + E_{i'} - mc \cdot \mathbf{1}[i' \neq i]}{\kappa_{\mathrm{loc}}}\Big).$$
  4. Fertility logit, only at fertile ages and only from $(n=0, \mathrm{cs}=0)$: $$V(b, t_o, i, 0, 0, j) = \kappa_{\mathrm{fert}} \log \sum_{n' = 0}^{n_{\mathrm{par}} - 1} \exp\!\big(V_I(b, t_o, i, n', \mathrm{cs}'(n'), j)/\kappa_{\mathrm{fert}}\big).$$

Outside fertile ages the fertility logit is skipped and $V \equiv V_I$. The whole recursion is implemented as four numba kernels per $(i, j)$ block.

2.4 Tenure choice in detail

Sale equity is $\mathrm{heq}[i, t_o] = (1-\psi)\, p_i\, H_{\mathrm{own}}[t_o - 1]$ (sales fee $\psi$). The buy/sell bookkeeping is non-trivial:

casenet wealth at $b'$feasibility
$t_o = t_n$ (stay)$b$—
$t_o = 0,\ t_n \geq 1$ (buy)$b - h_{\mathrm{cost}}$$b \geq (1-\phi) h_{\mathrm{cost}}$  and  $b - h_{\mathrm{cost}} \geq -\phi h_{\mathrm{cost}}$
$t_o \geq 1,\ t_n = 0$ (sell-and-rent)$\max(b + \mathrm{heq},\ 0)$—
$t_o \geq 1,\ t_n \geq 1,\ t_n \neq t_o$ (rebuy)$b + \mathrm{heq} - h_{\mathrm{cost}}$$b + \mathrm{heq} \geq (1-\phi) h_{\mathrm{cost}}$

Here $\phi$ is the financed share, so the down-payment threshold is $(1-\phi)\, h_{\mathrm{cost}}$. In the calibration $\phi = 0.80$.

2.5 Location logit

$$\Pr(i' \mid b, t_o, i, n, \mathrm{cs}) = \frac{\exp\!\big((V_{H,i'} + E_{i'} - mc \cdot \mathbf{1}[i' \neq i])/\kappa_{\mathrm{loc}}\big)}{\sum_{j'} \exp\!\big((V_{H,j'} + E_{j'} - mc \cdot \mathbf{1}[j' \neq i])/\kappa_{\mathrm{loc}}\big)}.$$

As $\kappa_{\mathrm{loc}} \to 0^{+}$, the logit collapses to deterministic argmax. At the calibrated value the implied annual mover share is on the order of a few percent, matching observed urban–rural migration rates.

Widget 3a — location logit temperature

Representative deterministic values $V_{H, i'} + E_{i'}$ are fixed; we just sweep $\kappa_{\mathrm{loc}}$ to show how the temperature reshapes the probability mass.

2.6 Fertility logit

At fertile ages, from a state with $n=0, \mathrm{cs}=0$:

$$\Pr(n' \mid b, i, j) = \frac{\exp(V_I(b, t_o, i, n', \mathrm{cs}'(n'), j)/\kappa_{\mathrm{fert}})}{\sum_{n''} \exp(V_I(b, t_o, i, n'', \mathrm{cs}'(n''), j)/\kappa_{\mathrm{fert}})}.$$

Once $n'$ is drawn the household commits: the parity state moves to $n'$ and the child stage advances on its own deterministic clock. There's no sequential second-birth hazard.

Widget 3b — fertility logit temperature

Representative $V_I$ values across $n' \in \{0,1,2,3\}$ — pre-set to favor $n' = 1$ slightly. Sweep $\kappa_{\mathrm{fert}}$ to see how a high temperature flattens the distribution toward Gumbel noise.

2.7 Bequest and terminal value

At the terminal age $j = J - 1$ the continuation value is the bequest function — lifetime housing is liquidated net of the transaction cost:

$$V_{\mathrm{bq}}(b, \mathrm{ten}, i, n) = \theta_0(n) \cdot \frac{(b + (1-\psi)p_i\, H_{\mathrm{own}}[\mathrm{ten}-1] + \theta_1)^{1-\sigma} - 1}{1-\sigma}, \qquad \theta_0(n) = 1 + \theta_n \cdot n.$$

The luxury-bequest term $\theta_1 \geq 0$ shifts the curvature near zero (De Nardi 2004). $\theta_n$ controls how much extra parents save for children — empirically calibrated to the ownership gap between parents and non-parents at retirement.

The kernels here are @njit(parallel=True) — the outer loop runs in prange(nc) over a flattened (n, cs) column index, while the inner $(b, t)$ block is sequential. That choice keeps writes race-free and avoids cross-thread synchronization.

4. How we solve it

4.1 Backward induction

The recursion runs from $j = J{-}1$ down to $j = 0$. At each age, four kernels fire in sequence: $V_d$ (savings + tenure-conditional), $V_H$ (tenure max), $V_I$ (location logsumexp), and $V$ (fertility logsumexp at fertile ages only). The animation traces the order.

4.2 Howard policy iteration

Inside the GE loop, the Bellman is fully resolved only every howard_freq iterations (or after stalls). Otherwise the savings policy $b'_{\text{stored}}$ is reused and only the period utility is re-evaluated at the stored $b'$ — the eval_* kernels. At the calibrated parameters this typically reduces inner-loop time about $3\times$.

do_full = force_full or it <= 3 or stored_bp is None \
       or (it % howard_freq == 0) or stall_count >= 2

if do_full:
    full Bellman, store bp
else:
    eval Bellman, plug in stored bp

4.3 Forward distribution

Mass propagates forward through five steps per period: fertility split → location split → tenure choice (deterministic) → savings scatter → child-stage advance. The forward kernel is single-threaded by design — mass updates accumulate, so parallel writes would race.

Two paths: a fast-stat compiled kernel that propagates only what the GE loop needs (population shares, housing demand, total births, mature entrants), and a full-stat Python pass that recomputes event-study moments at the accepted equilibrium only.

4.4 The general-equilibrium fixed point

Two loops nested: outer GE (prices $p$ and entry shares $e$), inner Bellman + forward. Prices update from housing-market excess demand; entry shares update from observed mature entrants by location. Convergence is tiered:

  • Hard: $\mathrm{err} < \mathrm{tol}_{eq}$.
  • Near-converged: $\mathrm{it} > 10$ and $\mathrm{best\_err} \leq \mathrm{tol}_{eq}$ (best across iterations, not necessarily current).
  • Soft-accept: $\mathrm{it} > 25$ and $\mathrm{best\_err} < 10\, \mathrm{tol}_{eq}$.
  • Cap: $\mathrm{it} > 50$ and $\mathrm{best\_err} < 0.02$.

The soft tiers exist because small-grid models with discrete tenure choice can oscillate around tenure thresholds rather than converge — a strict $\mathrm{err} < \mathrm{tol}_{eq}$ exit isn't always achievable. This matches the live MATLAB solver.

6. Calibration

  
The model is calibrated by simulated method of moments (SMM):

  $$\mathcal L(\theta) = \sum_{m \in \mathcal M} w_m \left(\frac{m^{\mathrm{model}}(\theta) - m^{\mathrm{target}}}{|m^{\mathrm{target}}|}\right)^{\!2},$$
  
over 17 moments with diagonal weights $w_m$ from parameters.py:build_calibration_setup. A separate inversion loop pins two amenity-side parameters $(E_C, r_{\bar{},C})$ to the Center population share and the Center–Periphery rent ratio.

6.2 Identification — which moment ties down which parameter

  
Every calibration is a system: many parameters move many moments, and identification has to argue which directions are nearly orthogonal. From CALIBRATION_PLAN_MERGED.md and our memory of past sweeps:

  
    
    
  • $\beta$ — discounted-utility curvature; identified by the young-age liquid wealth-to-income ratio.
    • 
    
  • $\chi$ — owner housing-services multiplier; identified by the aggregate ownership rate.
    • 
    
  • $\kappa_{\mathrm{loc}}$ — location logit temperature; identified by the cross-sectional dispersion of population across locations and the annual migration rate.
    • 
    
  • $\bar h_{\mathrm{jump}}$ — discrete housing floor jump at first birth; identified by the housing-increment-at-first-birth event-study target.
    • 
    
  • $\kappa_{\mathrm{fert}}$ — fertility logit temperature; identified jointly by TFR and the spread of $\Pr(n' \mid \cdot)$.
    • 
    
  • $\bar c_n,\ \bar h_n,\ \psi_{\mathrm{child}}$ — per-child commitment costs; identified by the fertility gradient with respect to housing costs and the parity-progression $1 \to 2$ moment.
    • 
    
  • $\theta_n$ — per-child bequest scaling; identified by the ownership gap between parents and non-parents at retirement.
    • 
  

  

    Caveat from memory: with maximum parity 2 and our current target list, TFR / childlessness / parity-progression are not independently targetable — there's an arithmetic constraint that ties their ratios. With $n_{\mathrm{par}} = 4$ in the live model, the constraint relaxes but doesn't vanish.
  


  
6.3 Geography inversion

  
The 13-vector $\theta$ controls the household side. Two amenity-side parameters $(E_C, r_{\bar{},C})$ control the spatial equilibrium and are pinned to two location-level moments by an inner inversion loop in objective.py: the Center population share and the Center–Periphery rent ratio. This separates structural identification (the household block) from spatial-equilibrium identification (the geography block) — useful both numerically (smaller outer search) and conceptually.

7.2 Stable gotchas

  
    
    
  • $\phi$ is the financed share, so the down-payment threshold uses $(1{-}\phi)\, h_{\mathrm{cost}}$.
    • 
    
  • parity_progression_1to2 is diagnostic-only in the live model; it is not part of the active target system.
    • 
    
  • $\bar c_n$ is a strong lever for TFR but kills parity-progression $1 \to 2$ because it hits the second-child margin directly.
    • 
    
  • Pre-MMS or pre-room-scale calibration losses are not directly comparable to live broad-core room-scale benchmark losses. Always check what target system a quoted loss is computed against.
    • 
    
  • The GE loop's soft-accept tier exists to handle oscillation around tenure thresholds. A strict $\mathrm{err} < \mathrm{tol}_{eq}$ exit is not always achievable on small grids.
